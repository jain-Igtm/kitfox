# Story Bible — Jane the Pilot

## Premise

Jane is a sixteen-year-old pilot who arrives on **St. Jude’s Head**, a remote Alaskan island, with a black modified Kitfox and a set of credentials that invite questions. **Jane is his surname.** The given name may remain withheld or undecided for now; narration and island personnel refer to him as Jane, which gives the name a slightly formal, file-label quality.

He is not merely pretending to be capable. He is pretending to be old enough and properly licensed for people to recognize the capability that already exists.

The story is not about a prodigy being admired. It is about a useful boy being absorbed by a place desperate enough to use him, cautious enough to resent him, and proud enough not to say either thing plainly.

## Jane

- Jane is his last name.
- Sixteen.
- A pilot and obsessive maintainer of his aircraft.
- Flies a black modified Kitfox.
- Hates shoes and avoids wearing them unless a situation makes the need practical rather than arbitrary.
- Flat, restrained, observant, and often dry in speech.
- Not warm in a conventional way, but capable of fierce attachment.
- Treats maintenance, accuracy, and usefulness as moral acts.
- Has serious medical knowledge and field experience but is not a doctor.
- Has a dangerous relationship with being needed; responsibility can feel like affection to him.

## Arrival conditions

Jane does **not** simply appear on St. Jude’s Head and get indulged.

He has corresponded with Ruth Calder before arriving. Ruth knows enough from emails, attachments, references, and aircraft documents to permit him to present himself in person, but not enough to trust him. He should be addressed in correspondence as **Mr. Jane**.

A serious operational problem is already happening when Jane arrives. The island’s normal air capability is compromised, the weather is closing or unstable, and the SAR group has reason to consider imperfect help. This pressure is what makes Jane’s presence possible.

Acceptance should be reluctant, provisional, and operational rather than sentimental. Ruth does not bless him; she uses him because the island is short on options and because he proves useful under pressure.

## Guardians

Jane was adopted by two eccentric, brilliant, emotionally cold but deeply present foster guardians. He called them by their first names.

### Mara

- Mathematician.
- Loved flying.
- Taught Jane mathematics, navigation, probability, weather, and rigorous thought.
- Cold but patient; affectionate through attention rather than softness.
- Died before Elias.

### Elias

- Doctor.
- Loved flying.
- After Mara died, he began extensive overseas medical volunteer work and brought Jane with him.
- Gave Jane a serious practical education in medicine through field clinics and crisis conditions.
- Trusted Jane with enormous responsibility.
- Died shortly before Jane arrived on St. Jude’s Head.

## Medical experience

Jane is not a licensed doctor and should not be framed as one. He has, however, done and seen more than many adults because of field conditions overseas with Elias.

He may plausibly know trauma care, wound management, infection control, pharmacology, anatomy, airway basics, shock, suturing, fracture stabilization, and the logistics of medicine under scarcity. He may have assisted procedures and sometimes been trusted with patient care under supervision or in constrained circumstances.

The tension should come from the space between real competence and legal permission.

## Aircraft

Jane’s aircraft is a **black modified Kitfox**.

The black finish should feel severe and practical rather than flashy. It may show rubbed contact points, dull repair patches, salt traces, oil fingerprints, and weathering. It is beautiful because it is cared for, not because it is polished.

The Kitfox should feel intimate, mechanical, and bodily: a light STOL-capable aircraft suited to rough strips, short hops, observation, improvised logistics, and island weather. Jane cares for it more reliably than he cares for himself.

## St. Jude’s Head

- Remote Alaskan island in the United States.
- Civilized but isolated.
- Has cliffs, forests, bad seas, harsh weather, limited infrastructure, and a small airfield that matters more than mainland officials understand.
- Not lawless. Rather, law exists alongside weather, scarcity, rescue urgency, liability, local reputation, and practical necessity.
- The setting should feel municipal and real: ferry schedules, Coast Guard coordination, state rules, FAA pressure, EMS liability, harbor authority, volunteer rescue politics, old equipment, grant-funded systems, damp buildings, and corroded metal.

## The island standard

St. Jude’s Head has an official flag or standard featuring an oddly militant old-style St. Jude figure. The locals are proud of it. It is displayed by search and rescue and appears on local aircraft, jackets, helmets, mugs, walls, and vehicles.

This should remain an observational texture, not a lore lecture.

Most pilots put the St. Jude emblem somewhere on their plane. Jane does not at first. No one gives him the patch, decal, or emblem. If he eventually adopts it, he gets it himself, privately and deliberately.

## Search and Rescue

The St. Jude’s Head SAR group is underfunded, competent in local ways, legally constrained, and constantly forced to balance procedure with necessity.

Important figures established so far:

### Ruth Calder

- Search and rescue chief or operations lead.
- Weathered, restrained, intelligent, difficult to impress.
- Notices what Jane does, not what he claims.
- Understands rules as both necessary and insufficient.
- Will not openly clear Jane quickly, but may create carefully limited spaces in which he can become useful.

### Wade

- Airport/SAR logistics/mechanic figure.
- Dry, practical, observant.
- Understands local infrastructure and equipment.
- Often sees Jane’s competence but also recognizes his danger.

## Tone

Dense, patient, materially grounded literary prose. Tension should emerge through weather, logistics, bodies, paperwork, machinery, and the behavior of institutions under strain. Avoid melodrama. Avoid over-explaining. Let authority operate through procedure, liability, maintenance, and silence.
