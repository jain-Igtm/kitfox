# Chapter Two — The Paper Door

Jane woke before the rain stopped.

For a few seconds he did not know where the roof was.

That happened sometimes after travel, and more often after bad weather. His mind woke inside the last useful room and then tested the world against it: canvas clinic, plywood ward, ferry bunk, motel ceiling, cockpit, hangar. He listened before moving. Rain on metal above him. A slow drip in the back corner. Wind forcing itself under a door that did not fit its frame. No generator. No voices. No rotor wash. No one vomiting into a basin. No one calling for Elias.

Hangar two.

St. Jude’s Head.

The black Kitfox stood above him in the dark, wing crossing the weak bulb light like a roof inside the roof. Its left tire was visible beyond the edge of the cot. The chock was still in place. The tie-down strap ran correctly through the floor ring, though he did not like the twist near the buckle. The aircraft had not moved in the night. The island had not required that of it.

Jane lay still until his eyes adjusted.

He had slept in his clothes. His sweater smelled of rain, avgas, wet wool, and a thin mineral trace from Mercy Cove that belonged to the old cannery strip. His shoes sat beneath the cot where he had taken them off without remembering doing it. One sock had dried folded against itself and would be unpleasant to put on. The other was missing. He found it under the blanket near his knee, stiff at the heel.

He sat up.

The medical kit was not in the hangar.

That was the first complete thought of the morning. Not the weather. Not Ruth Calder’s folder. Not the patient, Caleb, whose name returned with the hard clarity of a checked instrument. The kit was on the table in the rescue building because Ruth had told him to leave it there. He had obeyed, then disobeyed, and the shape of that contradiction had followed him into sleep.

He stood barefoot on the concrete.

Cold came up through his feet with useful cruelty. It told him where the floor was dry, where grit had gathered, where yesterday’s runoff had crossed the threshold and left a faint line of salt and pulverized gravel. Shoes lied by making all surfaces equally dull. Bare feet did not make him safer. They made fewer false promises.

He checked the aircraft before he checked himself.

Left brake fitting first. Still weeping, faintly. Not enough to make a puddle. Enough to be real. He touched the underside of the fitting with two fingers and rubbed the fluid between thumb and forefinger. Brake fluid, not water. No surprise. Surprise was usually the name people gave to information they had decided not to receive earlier.

He opened the notebook clipped to the cockpit and wrote:

Left brake fitting — confirmed seepage overnight. Inspect line / flare / fitting. Do not fly until addressed unless operational necessity exceeds risk and runway conditions permit minimal braking.

He looked at the last phrase for a long time.

Operational necessity exceeds risk.

That was the kind of sentence that had let him land at Mercy Cove. It was also the kind of sentence that could be used to kill people while sounding clean enough to survive a report.

He crossed it out.

Do not fly until addressed.

Better.

He made a slow circle around the Kitfox. Fabric, struts, leading edges, tires, tailwheel, control surfaces. Rain had worked along every seam and collected in the places he expected. A smear of Mercy Cove mud had dried along the inside of the left tire. There was grass caught at the tailwheel fork. He removed it carefully and set it on the floor instead of flicking it away, as though it were evidence.

It was evidence.

Everything was.

The hangar door rolled open before seven.

Jane turned. Wade stood in the gap with a paper cup in one hand and a small canvas tool roll in the other. The morning behind him was gray rather than dark. The airfield looked rinsed and unfinished: runway wet, fuel shed dark, the rescue building already lit, harbor fog lying low beyond the apron. Wade’s cap was damp at the brim. His beard had gone flat on one side from sleep or weather. He looked at the aircraft before he looked at Jane.

“Brake first,” Wade said.

Jane nodded.

Wade set the cup on the workbench, then reconsidered and moved it three inches away from a rust stain. He opened the tool roll. Wrenches, inspection mirror, flashlight, rag, small bottle, spare fittings, brake fluid. Not a complete kit. A kit assembled by someone who knew which incompleteness mattered.

“You sleep?” Wade asked.

“Yes.”

“How much?”

“Enough to wake.”

Wade accepted this without expression. “That answer gets worse the longer I think about it.”

“It was not intended to improve.”

For one second Wade’s face suggested he might answer in kind. He did not. He crouched by the left gear and pointed at the fitting.

“Show me what you saw.”

Jane crouched beside him and did.

They worked without speaking for several minutes. Wade cleaned the line, dried the fitting, had Jane depress the pedal slowly, then hold, then release. He watched the fitting through the mirror. A small bead formed at the flare.

“Not the line,” Wade said.

“No.”

“Flare or seat.”

“Yes.”

“You landed Mercy Cove with this.”

“I landed here with it first.”

Wade looked at him then.

Jane realized the answer had been defensive, therefore imprecise.

“I knew it was soft,” he said. “I did not know the source. I judged braking nonessential to the cove landing and manageable on return.”

“Was it?”

“Yes.”

“That doesn’t make the judgment clean.”

“No.”

Wade returned to the fitting. “Good. Don’t make me pull teeth every time the truth has dirt on it.”

Jane held the flashlight steady.

The workbench bulb hummed above them. Rain clicked softly on the hangar roof. Across the apron, the rescue building door opened and closed twice. A woman in a blue jacket carried a crate of radios inside. Someone had already restarted the day without asking whether the previous one was finished.

Wade loosened the fitting, inspected it, and handed Jane the mirror.

“Tell me.”

Jane angled the light. “Flare is slightly uneven. Seat may be scored.”

“May be?”

“Is. Small score at lower edge.”

“Repair?”

“Replace fitting. Reflare if line length allows. Otherwise line section.”

“Time?”

“Depends whether you have the fitting.”

Wade opened a small metal box and showed him three.

Jane looked at them, then at Wade.

Wade said, “Island rule. If it can strand you, own two.”

“That would mean owning more of everything.”

“Now you’re learning.”

The words were dry, but not light. He went back to the work.

After a while, Wade said, “At the cove. When did you decide?”

Jane kept the flashlight fixed. “After Aaron went down.”

“Not when the strip cleared?”

“No. When the strip cleared, landing became possible. When Aaron went down and Coast Guard lost the window, landing became relevant.”

Wade tightened the new fitting by hand first, then with the wrench. “Relevant is a dangerous word.”

“Yes.”

“You use a lot of those.”

Jane did not answer.

Wade leaned back on his heels. “I turned the radio down.”

“Yes.”

“That goes in the report.”

“Yes.”

“I don’t need you leaving that part out because I’m local and you’re not.”

“I wasn’t going to.”

“No. But you were going to answer only what got asked.”

Jane looked at him.

Wade’s face had lost all trace of humor. “This island runs on people answering only what got asked. It saves time until it doesn’t.”

Jane considered that.

Then he said, “You turned the radio down before I turned final.”

“Because I knew what you were about to do.”

“You could have stopped me.”

“I could have tried.”

The distinction sat between them.

Wade picked up the rag and wiped the fitting. “Ruth will not forgive the landing because it worked.”

“I didn’t expect her to.”

“Good. Do not ask her to. Do not make Wade told me to part of your report, either.”

Jane’s grip on the flashlight changed.

Wade noticed. “Because I didn’t. I made my own bad decision beside yours. They can stand separately.”

“That is not how reports usually read.”

“It is how this one will.”

Jane nodded once.

They bled the line. Wade made him do it twice, not because the second time changed much, but because the first repair after a hard landing deserved ceremony disguised as verification. The pedal firmed. Wade inspected again. No bead formed.

“Ground taxi later,” Wade said. “Not now.”

Jane wrote it down.

“Ruth has your kit,” Wade said.

Jane stopped writing.

“She did not throw it into the harbor.”

“I didn’t think she would.”

“You thought about whether she opened it.”

“Yes.”

“She did. With clinic on the phone. Inventory only.”

Jane finished the line in the notebook because stopping would make the information larger than it was.

Wade stood. His knees cracked quietly. “Debrief at eight. Bring your aircraft log. Bring your pilot documents. Bring whatever answer you plan to give when Ruth asks you why your paperwork makes everybody in the room unhappy.”

Jane closed the notebook.

“Is that the phrasing she used?”

“No,” Wade said. “She said worse.”

At 7:52, Jane crossed the apron with the folder Ruth had returned to the hangar door five minutes earlier through a volunteer who looked at Jane as if trying to decide whether he was famous, criminal, useful, or just wet. The folder was no longer in the same order. Ruth had inserted colored tabs along the edges: aircraft, pilot, medical, identity, incident.

Identity had a red tab.

He carried the folder under his left arm and the aircraft log under his right. His medical kit remained in the rescue building. The absence of it produced a constant pressure in his attention, like an alarm no one else could hear.

The morning had given St. Jude’s Head no kindness, only reduced force. The rain was thinner. Fog still held the harbor. The island standard hung damp beside the rescue building door, opening only when the wind came hard enough to force it. The saint appeared in fragments: hand, robe, severe face, blue field. Then the cloth folded again.

Inside, the building smelled of coffee, wet rope, toner, damp paper, and the disinfectant someone had used on the radio desk. The map wall had been cleaned but not cleared. Mercy Cove remained marked in red. A new timeline had been written on butcher paper and taped beside it.

15:18 — Initial Mercy Cove relay.

15:31 — Ridge Two reports Sisters Creek impassable.

15:46 — 734K reports visual.

16:22 — 734K lands St. Jude municipal.

16:39 — 734K departs with Wade for observation.

16:51 — Wade reports possible strip surface. IC orders no landing.

16:56 — 734K lands Mercy Cove.

The times continued down the paper with the pitiless calm of things that had already happened.

Ruth stood at the end of the table, not behind it. That mattered. A person behind a table was a judge. A person at the end of one was still part of the work. Ana sat at the radio desk with a yellow legal pad. Wade stood near the heater, arms folded, hair still wet from the hangar. A clinic physician joined by speakerphone. The Coast Guard pilot was not on the line; Ruth had already taken that statement. A harbor representative sat near the wall with a cap in his hands and the expression of a man who had been correct and still unhappy.

Ruth began when the second hand reached eight.

“This is an operational debrief and incident record. It is not a discipline meeting. If it becomes one, I will say so. We identify what happened, what was known at each decision point, and what must change before the next incident makes the same decision for us.”

No one spoke.

Her eyes moved to Jane. “You are here because you were part of the incident. Not because you are on the roster.”

“Yes.”

“That distinction will stay clear.”

“Yes.”

Ruth looked at Ana. “Start with radio traffic.”

Ana read from the log. Her voice was plain and steady. She did not perform the emergency. She reconstructed it. Each transmission became smaller in daylight, and therefore more dangerous. Emergencies, Jane had learned, often survived review by becoming too large to criticize. Ana did the opposite. Time. Sender. Message. Action taken. Unclear segments labeled unclear. Relayed medical information marked relay, not confirmed.

When she reached Jane’s first pass over Mercy Cove, Ruth stopped her.

“Mr. Jane. You gave the initial strip assessment. Repeat it now from memory.”

Jane did.

“Did you recommend landing?”

“No.”

“Why not?”

“Insufficient surface information. Unclear runoff damage. Driftwood hazard. Dirty wind. No ground signal controlling approach. No patient-loading plan.”

Ruth nodded once, not approval. Placement.

Ana continued.

When the timeline reached the second flight, Wade gave his report standing up. He did not embellish. Wire confirmed. Beach approach possible only from seaward side. Patient location. No safe road access. Ground party cleared debris. Possible landing surface. Ruth ordered no landing. Aaron went down. Coast Guard reported deteriorating ceiling. Radio volume turned down by Wade Halversen at approximately 16:54. Aircraft landed Mercy Cove approximately 16:56.

Ruth wrote that down herself.

Jane watched the pen move.

Wade did not look at him.

The clinic physician came on after Wade finished. Her name was Dr. Saye. Her voice had been thin the day before through radio relay; on the speakerphone it was still controlled but less distant, with fatigue underneath.

“I want to be precise about patient care,” she said. “Mr. Jane was not acting independently once I was in contact. He reported assessment findings clearly, distinguished confirmed from unknown, and followed direction. The improvised splint was appropriate under conditions. Thermal protection was appropriate. Movement was minimized. If he had attempted transport by fixed-wing, I would be giving a different statement.”

Ruth looked at Jane when that last sentence arrived.

He said nothing.

Dr. Saye continued. “Caleb is in Juneau. He has a head injury, closed femur fracture, exposure-related complications. He is alive. I am not going to speculate beyond that.”

The room received the word alive and did not celebrate it. Alive was not done. Alive was a door that remained open at cost.

“Aaron?” Ruth asked.

“Stable. Stitches, fluids, monitoring. Angry. Therefore improving.”

The harbor man closed his eyes briefly. Maybe he knew Aaron. Maybe everyone knew Aaron.

Ruth moved to the map. “Decision point. Ground party clears debris from old strip. Wade reports possible surface. I order continued observation and no landing. Thirty seconds later Aaron goes down and Coast Guard reports loss of ceiling. Mr. Jane, what did you know then?”

Jane stood because the question felt like it required the body arranged correctly.

“I knew the surface was longer than it appeared on the first pass. I did not know whether it would hold. I knew the west end was soft and the east end was cut. I knew a landing would not allow evacuation. I knew it would put us on scene before helicopter access failed completely. I knew Aaron was no longer fully functional. I knew the person with Caleb had limited communication and no visible equipment beyond what they had improvised. I knew my main medical kit was here.”

Ruth did not interrupt.

Jane continued because stopping there would make the report sound cleaner than the thought had been.

“I also knew I wanted to land.”

That changed the room more than the rest of it.

Ruth’s face did not move. “Because?”

“Because wanting to be useful is a bias.”

Wade looked up.

Ana stopped writing for half a second, then resumed.

Ruth said, “Did you correct for it?”

“I tried.”

“How?”

“I did not land on the first pass. I gave possible, not safe. I looked for reasons not to land after the strip cleared. I considered that landing would not evacuate the patient. I looked at Wade before turning final because he knew the cove and I did not.”

“That is not the same as authorization.”

“No.”

“Did Wade authorize you?”

“No.”

“Did he encourage you?”

“No.”

Wade said, “I turned the radio down.”

Ruth looked at him. “That is in the record.”

“I want it in this part of the record.”

“It is.”

Wade nodded.

Ruth turned back to Jane. “Did you believe delay would materially worsen the patient’s outcome?”

“Yes.”

“Based on what?”

“Exposure. Altered mentation. Mechanism. Possible long-bone fracture. No road access. Boat unavailable. Coast Guard window closing. Loss of reliable communication at the cove. No visible patient packaging or thermal gear from the pass.”

Dr. Saye said, “That reasoning is medically sound. It does not answer the aviation question.”

“No,” Jane said.

Ruth said, “Answer the aviation question.”

“The landing was within the aircraft’s capability and near the edge of acceptable judgment. The takeoff was worse. I would not put it in a procedure. I would not call it safe.”

“What would you call it?”

Jane looked at the Mercy Cove map.

“Successful,” he said. “Which is less useful.”

For the first time that morning, Ruth seemed to find exactly the answer she had been looking for and dislike needing it.

“Yes,” she said. “It is.”

The debrief lasted another hour.

No one became theatrical. That made it harder, not easier. Competent people did not always spare one another by being kind. Sometimes competence meant refusing the mercy of confusion. The harbor man described the sea state in exact local terms: outer teeth breaking white, rebound off Black Friar, cross-set chop at the hook, no safe line for a rescue boat under that wind. Ana noted radio failure points. Wade listed equipment gaps: portable repeater battery rotation, wire hazard not marked on current air ops map, old cannery strip not classified, no current surface report. Dr. Saye requested a sealed field kit staged at SAR, not locked in the clinic, with authority language attached before the next incident forced improvisation.

Ruth wrote all of it down.

Jane had expected anger to be the hard part. Anger was simple. This was worse. The island was not punishing him. It was absorbing him into paperwork.

Paperwork had always looked stupid to people who had never watched it decide whether a thing could happen. Elias had kept forms in waterproof bags. Mara had labeled documents with a pencil so sharp the letters seemed machined. A signature could open a border or close a ward. A date could let medication pass customs or leave it in a hot room until it spoiled. A missing stamp could make a competent person illegal and an incompetent one official.

After the debrief, Ruth did not dismiss him with the others.

“Office,” she said.

Wade came too because Ruth pointed at him without looking.

The office was narrow and glass-walled, with a dehumidifier humming in the corner and binders shelved according to a system that had probably survived three coordinators. The window looked out on the apron. Hangar two sat beyond the fuel pump, its door half open. The black Kitfox was not visible from this angle. Jane disliked that.

Ruth set his folder on the desk.

“Sit.”

Jane sat.

Wade remained standing beside the door.

Ruth opened the folder to the aircraft tab first. Registration. Insurance. Maintenance log. Modification list. Propeller notes. Fabric repairs. Avionics work. She read not like someone searching for a mistake but like someone building the shape of a thing she might one day have to defend.

“The aircraft is not my first problem,” she said.

Jane waited.

“That does not mean it is not a problem. Wade gets a full inspection before it flies in connection with anything happening here.”

“Yes.”

Wade said, “Brake’s fixed. I still want the gear, control cables, fuel system, and attach points.”

Jane nodded.

Ruth moved to the pilot tab.

There, the air changed.

She did not announce what she saw. She turned one page, then another, then set a photocopied document beside an original, then looked at the identification card under the desk lamp. Her expression did not sharpen. It became more still.

“These documents do not all tell the same story,” she said.

“No.”

Wade’s eyes moved from Ruth to Jane.

Ruth tapped one page lightly with the back of her pen. “Some of this may be explainable.”

“Yes.”

“Some of it may be verifiable but insufficient.”

“Yes.”

“And some of it is not a paperwork problem. It is a calendar problem.”

Jane looked at the wall behind her, where a small framed photograph showed a winter rescue from years before: three people kneeling beside a litter in snow, all of them turned away from the camera toward the patient. No one in the image appeared proud.

“Yes,” he said.

Ruth closed the pilot tab but kept her hand on it.

“Do not mistake what I am asking for,” she said. “I did not request your paperwork because I was looking for a reason to send you away. I requested it because once a person becomes useful, everyone starts pretending the door is wider than it is.”

Jane looked at her then.

“The door is not wide,” she continued. “The door is paperwork, law, insurance, medical direction, command authority, weather, mechanical condition, and the judgment of people who are tired and frightened and very good at lying to themselves after the fact.”

Wade was quiet by the door.

Ruth said, “Yesterday I used you. Then you exceeded the assignment. Then a patient benefited. All three things are true. None of them cancel the others.”

“I know.”

“I don’t think you know yet. I think you know facts. Knowing facts is not the same thing as living under the structure that connects them.”

Jane had no answer that was not pride wearing a better coat.

Ruth opened the medical tab.

Elias’s letter sat near the top. Jane had known it would. It had been written on clinic stationery and scanned badly at the edges. Elias’s handwriting was narrow, abrupt, and economical, with one correction in the margin where he had changed assisted to performed under direct supervision and then changed it back again to assisted because Elias had believed accuracy mattered more than generosity.

Ruth read the letter again. She had read it the night before. Jane could tell by the way her eyes did not slow at the same places. Wade looked out the glass wall and pretended not to read the reflection.

“Elias Venn,” Ruth said.

“Yes.”

“Doctor. Guardian.”

“Yes.”

“Dead six weeks.”

“Yes.”

Wade’s jaw moved once.

Ruth placed the letter down carefully, separate from the other papers.

“I am sorry,” she said.

Jane nodded.

The words had no place to go, so they remained in the office with the damp binders and the humming dehumidifier.

Ruth continued. “Dr. Saye is willing to speak with you later today. Not to clear you. To understand what you know and what you think you know. Those are different categories.”

“Yes.”

“You will answer her directly.”

“Yes.”

“You will not make her drag information out of you because you dislike being assessed.”

Jane looked down at his hands.

“That is not an accusation,” Ruth said. “It is an instruction based on observing you for less than twenty-four hours.”

“Yes.”

Wade made a small sound near the door, not amusement exactly. Recognition again.

Ruth closed the folder.

“Here is where we are. You are not on the SAR roster. You are not medical staff. You are not authorized for patient transport. You are not authorized to carry my people in your aircraft during an incident. You are not authorized to self-dispatch because your airplane is fast and your instincts were useful once.”

Jane absorbed the list without moving.

“But,” Ruth said, and the word was not mercy, “I am not throwing away a useful person in a place that has too few of them. Pending review, you may assist Wade with maintenance, logistics, map updates, weather observations, and ground support. You may sit radio log under Ana if she agrees to tolerate you. You may speak to Dr. Saye about medical knowledge, under her terms. Your aircraft stays grounded until Wade signs off on it mechanically, and even then it is your aircraft, not our aircraft. If that distinction blurs, I stop this before it becomes a habit.”

Jane said, “Understood.”

Ruth studied him.

“No,” she said. “Accepted is enough for now.”

He corrected himself. “Accepted.”

She pushed the folder back toward him, except for three pages she kept: copies, not originals. Identity. Pilot document. Elias’s letter.

“I will make calls,” she said. “Some may go badly.”

“Yes.”

“If you leave before I finish making them, that becomes information.”

“I won’t.”

Ruth did not ask him to promise. Competent people knew the difference between a promise and an observed pattern.

She looked at Wade. “Hangar two?”

Wade said, “Roof leaks aft corner. Door binds left. Electrical is ugly but not immediately murderous. He found the dry patch.”

“Of course he did.”

Jane did not know what to do with that sentence.

Ruth stood. “Tonight also. Not indefinite. He gets a carbon monoxide detector, a fire extinguisher that isn’t older than he is, and he does not run a stove in there.”

“I don’t have a stove,” Jane said.

“Good. Continue not having one.”

This was almost a joke. It did not feel like one. It felt like a person making a rule before a body could become evidence.

When the office door opened, the building noise returned: radio static, the heater, a printer starting and stopping, wet boots on the floor. Ana looked up from the radio desk as Jane came out.

“Ruth says you might sit log,” she said.

“If you agree.”

“I don’t agree yet.”

“Yes.”

She pointed at the chair beside her. “Sit anyway. We’ll see how annoying you are when no one is actively dying.”

Jane sat.

The phrase should have bothered him. It did not. People who worked around death often spoke of it without reverence because reverence took time and time was not always available. What mattered was whether they became careless. Ana did not seem careless.

She placed a blank log sheet in front of him.

“Rules,” she said. “Time first. Source second. Message third. Action fourth. If you don’t hear it, you write unclear. If you infer it, you mark inferred. If you think something is obvious, you still write it down like the next person is exhausted and hates you.”

Jane looked at the sheet.

“Yes.”

Ana watched him for a moment. “You already know this.”

“Yes.”

“Clinic?”

“Some.”

“Elias?”

He did not answer immediately.

Ana did not apologize for the question. She also did not push it forward.

“Yes,” Jane said.

She nodded and keyed the radio. “St. Jude station, radio check on portable three.”

A volunteer answered from somewhere near the fuel shed. Ana had Jane write it. Then she corrected the spacing. Then the abbreviation. Then the time format.

“You write like a person trying to make the paper proud,” she said.

Jane adjusted the next line.

“Better,” Ana said.

By noon, he had logged radio checks, a weather update, the return of Ridge Two gear, a clinic supply request, a harbor notice, and one call about a loose goat near Old Cannery Road that everyone in the building ignored until Ana marked it non-SAR and passed it to municipal.

The island did not stop being strange because no one was bleeding in front of him.

At 12:17, Dr. Saye arrived instead of calling.

She came in through the side door carrying a waterproof medical bag and wearing a dark green jacket with the clinic patch at the shoulder. She was in her forties, maybe older, with black hair threaded in silver and the pale, focused exhaustion of a person who had slept badly in a room with fluorescent lights. Ruth intercepted her near the map wall. They spoke briefly, low. Dr. Saye looked at Jane once. Not curiously. Clinically.

“Mr. Jane,” she said.

He stood.

“Bring a chair.”

The clinic interview took place at the far end of the main room, not in Ruth’s office. Jane understood why. Visible, but not public. Ruth worked at the map wall within hearing if anyone raised a voice. Wade was outside. Ana wore her headset and pretended not to listen with the discipline of someone listening professionally to three other things.

Dr. Saye opened a notebook.

“I’m not here to decide whether you’re impressive,” she said.

“Good.”

“I’m here because yesterday I gave medical direction over radio to someone who was not on our roster and not credentialed in any way I had verified. That cannot become normal just because the first outcome was acceptable.”

“Yes.”

“What training have you had?”

Jane told her.

Not all of it. Not at first. He began with the clean parts: wilderness first aid, EMT coursework that had not resulted in a current state credential, field clinic exposure, Elias’s supervision, logistics, wound care, splinting, infection control, patient monitoring. Dr. Saye wrote without interrupting. When he used a vague phrase, she stopped him.

“Assisted how?”

He clarified.

“Observed or performed?”

“Both, depending.”

“Depending is not an answer.”

He clarified again.

“Who documented it?”

“Elias. Sometimes the clinic lead.”

“Can those records be obtained?”

“Some.”

“Some because they don’t exist, or some because you don’t want them found?”

Jane looked at her.

Dr. Saye did not soften the question.

“Both,” he said.

She wrote that down.

The interview moved through anatomy, shock, hypothermia, airway positioning, bleeding control, fracture stabilization, medication boundaries, consent, scope. She did not ask him to perform competence. She asked where he would stop. The stopping points mattered more to her than the knowledge.

“What would you not do without direction?”

“Medication beyond basic over-the-counter unless specifically ordered. Invasive airway. Reduction. Anything requiring sterile field I don’t have. Anything that substitutes for evacuation when evacuation exists.”

“What would you do if alone and evacuation did not exist?”

“That is not the same question.”

“No,” Dr. Saye said. “It is the question that tells me whether the first answer was memorized.”

Jane looked toward the map wall.

Ruth was reading a form. She was not looking at him. Wade had come back inside and stood near the gear racks, speaking quietly to a volunteer about rope that had been dried wrong.

Jane answered the second question.

He did not make it heroic. He did not make it clean. He said what he would attempt, what he would avoid, what he would need, what would change his mind, where the patient would likely die anyway, and what he would document if there were paper and time.

Dr. Saye listened.

When he finished, she closed the notebook halfway.

“You have real knowledge,” she said.

Jane felt the sentence arrive and refused to let it become warmth.

“You also have gaps,” she continued. “Some are training gaps. Some are authority gaps. Some are personality gaps.”

“Yes.”

“You say yes quickly.”

“Yes.”

“That is one of them.”

He stopped.

Dr. Saye leaned back. “Here is my line. In this building, you do not represent clinic. You do not represent EMS. You may assist under direct instruction if I or a designated medical lead gives it. You may inventory supplies, carry equipment, record, observe, and shut up when someone with license and liability tells you to shut up.”

Jane nodded.

“If you see something dangerous, you say it. Once. Clearly. Not as a performance. Not as a challenge. As information.”

“Yes.”

“If the lead hears you and continues, you do not start a second command structure unless someone is about to be harmed by the instruction itself.”

Jane looked at her.

“That sentence is where adults get into trouble,” Dr. Saye said. “Do not enjoy it.”

“I don’t.”

“I believe that less than I’d like.”

The interview ended there.

Dr. Saye stood and went to Ruth. They spoke beside the map wall. Jane could hear nothing but tone. Professional. Low. Serious. Not hostile. That was worse than hostile. Hostility could be dismissed. Seriousness had to be carried.

Ana slid a radio log toward him with two fingers.

“Portable four check,” she said.

Jane wrote it down.

By late afternoon, the rain had become mist. St. Jude’s Head emerged in layers beyond the windows: harbor roofs, cranes, the ferry ramp, spruce dark on the slope, gulls fixed in the wind above the dump road, the runway shining black under low cloud. The island looked less like resistance from the ground. It looked like labor.

Wade found Jane at the log desk at 16:40.

“Aircraft inspection,” he said.

Ana looked at Ruth. Ruth nodded without looking up from a call. Jane stood.

The inspection took two hours.

Wade did not let him assist at first. He made Jane watch him inspect the aircraft as though it belonged to someone else. That, Jane understood, was the point. People were blind to the things they loved by touch. Wade checked the brake repair, gear attach points, tailwheel, control cables, pulleys, fabric, strut fittings, fuel drains, filters, electrical routing, baggage restraints, propeller, engine mounts, cowling fasteners, firewall, tires, ELT, survival gear, and the place behind the seat where Jane had wedged extra supplies too neatly.

“You trust your packing,” Wade said.

“Yes.”

“You trust it because you did it.”

“Yes.”

“That is not a reason.”

“It is a reason. It is not enough.”

Wade looked at him for a second. “Better.”

He found three things he disliked and one thing he liked enough not to say so. The disliked things were marked with blue tape. The liked thing he touched twice and moved on from.

At the end, he signed nothing.

“Mechanically,” Wade said, “it is a good aircraft.”

Jane waited.

“You maintain it like somebody will die if you don’t.”

“Someone might.”

“Yes,” Wade said. “That’s the healthy half of the thought.”

“What is the other half?”

“That if somebody dies, maintenance can save you from it being your fault.”

Jane looked at the left wing.

Wade closed the inspection panel. “It can’t.”

The hangar held the sentence after him.

Wade packed his tools, then paused at the door.

“Ruth will not clear you quickly.”

“I know.”

“She may never clear you the way you want.”

Jane said nothing.

“You should decide now whether you are here to be useful or to be recognized.”

“That is not a clean distinction.”

“No. It is the dirtiest one we have.”

Wade left.

Jane stood alone beside the Kitfox in the gray light. Across the apron, the rescue building had begun to glow again as evening moved in. The medical kit sat on his workbench now. Dr. Saye had returned it sealed with blue tape and a handwritten inventory sheet clipped to the handle. Items used from the cockpit kit were listed separately. Replacement required. Reimbursement pending. Nothing sentimental. Nothing missing.

He broke the tape and opened it.

Everything was arranged almost correctly. Not his order. Close. Dr. Saye’s order, maybe. Practical, but not intimate. He removed each item, checked it, and put it back where his hand would expect it under stress. Dressings. Gloves. Airway roll. SAM splint. Tourniquets. Trauma shears. Hemostatic gauze. Thermal blankets. Tape. Marker. Saline. Medications sealed and labeled. He checked expiration dates. He checked packaging. He checked the empty spaces.

Then he wrote a replacement list for the cockpit kit.

Small shears.

Compressed gauze.

Tape.

Space blanket.

Nitrile gloves.

Triangular bandage.

Duct tape — aircraft pouch.

Wool blanket x2 — used / replace.

He looked at the last line and thought of Caleb’s face in the rain, eyes open without finding him.

Alive, Dr. Saye had said.

Alive was not absolution. It was only a door left open.

He took Elias’s oilcloth-wrapped notebook from under the cot and sat on the concrete with his back against the workbench. For a while he did not open it. The notebook had weight beyond paper. Elias had written in narrow black ink, often without dates, often with abbreviations that assumed anyone reading had been present or should have been. Jane had been present for some of it. Too much of it. Not enough.

He opened to a page near the middle.

Do not confuse composure with control.

Below it, in smaller writing:

The patient does not benefit from your need to be the sort of person who does not panic. Do the next indicated thing.

Jane read the lines twice.

Outside, someone crossed the apron laughing once, then stopped as if the sound had surprised them. A truck started near the fuel shed. The island standard cracked in a gust and then fell slack again.

Jane closed the notebook.

He wrote one more line beneath the replacement list.

Tomorrow: ask what the next indicated thing is before deciding alone.

He stared at it until the words became unattractive enough to keep.

Then he put the medical kit under the workbench, not in the aircraft. Not yet. The aircraft did not need it tonight. The room did. The distinction mattered because he did not like it.

When he lay down, the black wing held the last of the hangar light above him. Rain began again after dark, soft at first, then steadier. Across the apron the rescue building remained lit. People moved inside it, not welcoming him, not rejecting him, not yet able to decide what the island had taken in.

Jane listened until the sounds separated themselves into systems.

Rain.

Roof.

Wind.

Radio through a wall he could not hear but knew was speaking.

The Kitfox cooling into night.

The island continuing.

For now, continuation was enough.
