# Chapter One — St. Jude’s Head

By the time St. Jude’s Head answered him, Jane had been listening to the island fail for twenty-three minutes.

Not collapse. Collapse was dramatic and therefore usually imprecise. The island was not collapsing. It was continuing to exist under conditions that made each ordinary system reveal what it had always depended on: a road shoulder, a working culvert, a boat that could round the head, a helicopter ceiling, a radio repeater with enough battery, one pilot not sick, one mechanic not waiting on a part that had been promised from Juneau three days ago and still sat in a warehouse because fog had become a civic authority.

St. Jude’s Head was failing in pieces, and everyone on the frequency knew the pieces by name.

“Station, Ridge Two. Sisters Creek is gone. Roadbed’s undercut on both sides. We can’t get the truck across.”

The voice came through rain static and engine noise, flattened by a handheld radio that had been held too close to a wet jacket. Jane listened from eight hundred feet over gray water with one hand light on the stick and the other resting below the throttle. The black Kitfox moved through the weather without resentment. Its wings took the rain as a condition, not an insult. When gusts came down out of the cloud-wrapped ridge ahead, the struts trembled, the left wing lifted, and the aircraft told him about the air before any instrument could make the message polite.

“Can you continue on foot?” a woman asked.

Her voice had been the center of the frequency since he first picked it up north of Cape Spencer. Calm, severe, economical. She did not soften questions because the answers might be bad.

“Negative with the litter,” Ridge Two said. “Creek’s too high. We have washout, standing water, and moving debris. If the patient’s still at Mercy Cove, road access is done.”

Jane looked west.

The island appeared in pieces ahead of him. Spruce. Cliff. Fog. A white blow of surf at the outer rocks. Then nothing but rain. On the chart, St. Jude’s Head had looked almost deliberate: one remote Alaskan island bent around a hard western promontory, one municipal runway cut onto a bench above the harbor, one north road fading toward old cannery ground and private tracks whose names varied by map. From the air it looked less like land than resistance. Every usable surface had been argued out of slope, weather, cost, water, and the stubbornness of people who did not want to leave.

The clinic came on next.

“Station, clinic. I need an update from Mercy Cove. Last relay said possible femur fracture, scalp laceration, altered mentation, exposure. If the road team can’t reach them, I need to know what transport option we still have.”

“Understood,” the woman said.

It was not a comforting word. Nothing in her voice was made for comfort. She made understood sound like a tool set down flat where it could be found again.

Jane adjusted two degrees west to keep the ridge shadow from pulling him lower than he intended. Mercy Cove lay somewhere beyond the next rain band, under the north slope and east of the old cannery. He had marked it on his paper chart in blue pencil three nights earlier, then again in black after three sources contradicted one another about whether the old strip behind the cannery still existed. One satellite image showed gravel. Another showed grass. A post on a fishing forum from six years ago claimed a Super Cub had landed there in a crosswind and that no honest man would try it twice. The poster had used three exclamation points and misspelled aileron. Jane had still copied the note.

Bad information could contain good fear.

The harbor office broke in, voice older and closer to anger than panic.

“Station, harbor. I am telling you again, no boat rounds the head in this. Not ours, not private, not somebody’s nephew with a crab skiff. Water’s breaking across the outer teeth.”

“Copy, harbor.”

“You copy the words, Ruth, but I want the room to understand the meaning.”

“I understand the meaning.”

There it was. Ruth. Ruth Calder, then. St. Jude’s Head Search and Rescue coordinator, operations lead, author of twelve emails and one unanswered voicemail. Jane had read her messages often enough to know their shape. Direct. Complete sentences. No apology for limits. No friendliness used as padding. The first reply had come thirty-one hours after his initial inquiry.

Mr. Jane,

St. Jude’s Head Search and Rescue is not hiring pilots. We are reviewing volunteer support for ground, logistics, radio, and maintenance only. If you intend to present yourself in person, bring aircraft registration, maintenance logs, proof of insurance, pilot documentation, medical references, and identification. Do not construe this as acceptance.

He had not construed it as acceptance. He had construed it as a door whose lock had not yet been tested.

His final email had said he would arrive no later than Friday, weather permitting. It had not said he would avoid Thursday if Thursday became possible. The difference mattered. Ruth Calder, he suspected, would dislike that it mattered.

The Kitfox crossed another seam of air. Rain struck the windshield in grains, then threads, then sheets. Jane lowered his eyes to the instruments only long enough to verify what his hand already knew: altitude steady, airspeed honest, engine temperatures normal, fuel sufficient, oil pressure good. The left brake had felt slightly soft during the stopover at Yakutat. It would matter on the ground, not here. He had noted it in the black book clipped to the panel.

He changed frequency long enough to make the call he owed the field.

“St. Jude traffic, experimental seven-three-four kilo, Kitfox, ten east-northeast, eight hundred, inbound St. Jude municipal, listening advisory.”

For three seconds, no one answered.

Then a man came on, dry and local. “Seven-three-four kilo, St. Jude advisory. Wind two-eight-zero at one-eight, gusting two-six. Altimeter two-niner-eight-four. Runway three-zero favored. Active SAR north side. No local air asset available.”

Jane wrote the numbers into the airplane with his hand and feet. “Seven-three-four kilo copies. Runway three-zero.”

A second voice entered before the man could continue.

“Seven-three-four kilo, say pilot name.”

Ruth.

Jane kept his eyes outside. “Jane.”

A pause followed. Not confusion exactly. Reclassification.

“Mr. Jane.”

“Yes.”

“You were expected tomorrow.”

“I said no later than tomorrow.”

“This is not a good time for precision.”

Jane did not answer. She was right in one sense and wrong in the only sense that mattered to flight. There was no version of that distinction that would improve the next thirty seconds.

“Continue to St. Jude municipal,” Ruth said. “Remain clear of north-side operations.”

The rain band thinned ahead of him.

The north shore opened suddenly below the left wing: black rock, pale surf, water torn white where the cove narrowed against the hooked arm of the head. The old cannery appeared as rusted roofs and one collapsed wall behind a strip of gray beach. Above it, where the road should have approached through spruce and alder, a brown scar ran down the slope and disappeared into floodwater.

Mercy Cove.

Jane did not move at once. Wanting to be useful altered scale. It made short things look long, gaps look passable, weather look temporary, and rules look like the work of people who had not seen the exact thing you were seeing. Elias had hated that in him before Jane knew it required hatred. Mara had named it more precisely: eagerness pretending to be analysis.

He held altitude.

The old strip lay behind the cannery, parallel to the beach. It was worse than the chart and better than rumor. Gravel showed through grass in pale scabs. Driftwood had collected along the seaward edge. The west end was wet. The east end had been cut by runoff. The middle, though, existed. It did not look safe. It looked like a fact that would tempt unsafe people.

Jane keyed the mic.

“Station, seven-three-four kilo has Mercy Cove in sight.”

Ruth answered immediately. “Do not divert.”

“I have the washout in sight.”

“Continue municipal.”

“Old cannery strip visible. I can give you one pass. Offshore. No landing.”

There was a pause long enough to contain several better worlds.

“Six hundred minimum,” Ruth said. “One pass. Do not land.”

“Seven-three-four kilo copies. One pass. Six hundred minimum.”

He put the left wing slightly down and let the cove travel through the side window.

The rain made everything uncertain, then, in small mercies, briefly exact. A white pickup sat on the wrong side of the washout with the driver’s door open. Two figures were near the cannery wall, one upright, one kneeling. A third shape lay on a tarp or sheet of plastic beside them. Orange showed at shoulder height: a jacket, not a signal panel. The standing figure waved with both arms, broad and desperate. Behind him, the strip ran dull and broken through grass.

Jane did not descend.

“Station, seven-three-four kilo. Visual on three persons at Mercy Cove near cannery wall. One down on tarp. One kneeling. One standing. White pickup east of washout, inaccessible from road. Old strip partly visible. Middle section appears clear in places, approximately three hundred feet, maybe less. Driftwood seaward side. West end wet. East end damaged by runoff. Not recommending fixed-wing landing from this pass.”

Ruth repeated the essentials into another channel. He could hear her voice turn from receiving to distributing. She did not praise information. She moved it.

The clinic came in. “Can aircraft see patient movement?”

Jane made a shallow turn, careful not to tighten it into the ridge wind. “Standing person moving. Kneeling person moving. Patient not visibly moving from this altitude.”

The clinic did not answer for half a second.

Then: “Copy. Request relay to Coast Guard.”

Ruth returned. “Seven-three-four kilo, continue municipal.”

Jane looked once more at the strip.

Three hundred feet was not enough, except when it was. The Kitfox could do ugly things in small places if the pilot did not ask it to forgive stupidity. The surface was unknown. The wind was wrong. The approach crossed rough air over the cannery and gave no clean exit after a point that would arrive quickly and without drama. It was not a runway. It was a proposition made by bad weather to a person who wanted to matter.

He turned away.

“Seven-three-four kilo continuing municipal.”

As the cove slid behind the wing, the standing figure kept waving. Not at him, probably. At the sound. At anything that appeared to be leaving.

Jane did not look back after the turn. Looking back after deciding was one of the cheaper ways to undo a decision without admitting it.

St. Jude’s municipal appeared eight minutes later between moving curtains of rain. The runway lay dark on a cut bench above the harbor. A fuel shed stood beside a fenced pump. Three hangars faced the apron in a row, each repaired by a different decade and none of them fully winning its argument with the island. The search-and-rescue building was low, antenna-bristled, and lit from within. Beside it, the island standard snapped hard in the wind: dark blue field, white border, an old severe figure of St. Jude with one arm raised. From the air the saint looked less devotional than official, a symbol chosen by people who expected lost causes to fill out paperwork and keep moving.

Two local aircraft sat beside the first hangar. One with its cowling open. One blocked by a maintenance stand. The absence they created was visible before Jane touched down.

He landed with more runway than pride wanted and less than caution preferred. Wet pavement hissed under the tires. The crosswind pressed, shifted, pressed again. He let the tail settle and kept the rollout clean, aware of the left brake without needing it. At the first taxiway he called clear and brought the aircraft toward the apron where a man in a rain jacket waited with no hood up, as if the island had already worn that argument out of him.

Wade Halversen, likely. The name from Ruth’s email and the hangar invoice. The man who had written, below the payment instructions: Door binds left. Lift before rolling. Don’t force it or I’ll know.

Wade signaled him toward the rescue building, not the hangar.

Jane shut down in the open. The engine ticked as it cooled. Rain collected immediately along the black cowling seam and ran toward the fasteners in thin bright lines.

For three seconds after the prop stopped, Jane remained seated.

The cockpit smelled of damp fabric, oil, old chart paper, and the metallic trace of weather brought inside on sleeves and skin. Without the engine, the island became louder. Rain. Wind against struts. A truck downshifting on the harbor road. The distant, irregular pulse of surf below the field.

He unclipped the black notebook, wrote the landing time, and stepped down barefoot onto the wet apron.

Wade looked at his feet, then at his face, then at the aircraft. He did not waste an expression on any of them.

“Mr. Jane.”

“Jane is fine.”

Wade took that as information, not permission. “Ruth wants you inside.”

“The airplane needs securing.”

“Not before the map.”

Jane looked past him. Through the rescue building windows he could see bodies gathered around one wall, a radio operator leaning forward, someone with a phone pressed to her shoulder, one man in orange gear standing too still with both hands on his hips. The aircraft, unsecured, could become another problem. The room already was one.

Wade saw him choose.

“Chocks,” Wade said. “Fast.”

They did it together. Not properly. Enough. Wade set the first chock with his boot, then crouched and corrected it with his hand when he saw where Jane was looking. That improved him. Jane strapped the wing with a temporary angle that would need changing when the wind shifted. He hated leaving it that way. He hated that leaving it was correct.

He opened the baggage compartment and removed the plastic-wrapped folder.

His hand paused over the medical kit.

“Bring it,” Wade said.

Jane did.

The rescue building was hot, wet, and almost silent for a place filled with activity. People spoke only when the words had somewhere to go. Maps covered the largest wall: tide marks, cliff access routes, radio shadows, old wreck sites, culvert notes, property boundaries, informal names layered over official ones in grease pencil. Radios occupied a long desk below a framed version of the island standard. Helmets, flotation suits, throw bags, harnesses, medical packs, and rain jackets hung from hooks and shelves with labels made by people who had learned that organization was cheaper than replacement.

Ruth Calder stood at the map wall with a pencil in one hand and a phone trapped between shoulder and ear. She was shorter than her emails, which happened often with people whose writing carried no wasted motion. Her hair was wet at the temples, cut close, silver-gray. She wore an orange SAR jacket faded at the elbows and a dark sweater beneath it with sleeves pushed up. She looked at Jane once, taking in his face, folder, bare feet, and the case in his hand, and returned to the phone.

“No,” she said. “If the road is gone, the road is gone. I need current ceiling at the cove, not what the automated station thinks is happening at the harbor.”

A woman at the radio desk lifted two fingers without looking away from her headset. “Coast Guard wants patient status.”

“So do I,” Ruth said. “Tell them we have eyes from an inbound aircraft and no ground access yet.”

The radio operator repeated this more politely.

Ruth hung up and turned to Jane.

For a moment neither of them spoke.

Jane recognized the pause. It existed in clinics when someone arrived with exactly the thing needed and several reasons not to use it. It existed in hangars when a part fit but had no paperwork. It existed anywhere reality moved faster than permission.

“You saw the strip,” Ruth said.

“Yes.”

“Would you land there?”

“Not from that pass.”

“Why?”

“Surface unknown. West end wet. East end cut by runoff. Wind over the cannery is dirty. Patient loading would be slow.”

Wade, behind him, shifted his weight. Not approval. Recognition.

Ruth pointed at the map. “Show me exactly what you saw.”

Jane put his shoes on before she told him to. They were in the side pocket of his duffel, still damp from the ferry he had taken that morning to inspect hangar two before bringing the aircraft over. The floor became less informative immediately.

Mercy Cove was marked in red. So was Sisters Creek, where the road had failed. Someone had drawn a line toward the cannery and crossed it out twice. Jane took the pencil Ruth handed him and waited half a second, long enough to be sure she intended him to mark her map. Then he bracketed the old strip’s usable-looking middle, marked the pickup, the washout, the driftwood line, the three people near the cannery wall, and the likely wire hazard from pump house to building corner.

Ruth noticed the wire mark.

“You saw that?”

“No. I inferred it from the mast and pump house.”

Wade stepped closer. “There is a line there. Dead, or supposed to be. Still physically present last time I was in.”

Ruth nodded once. “Good. Mark it.”

Jane darkened the line.

The radio spoke with Coast Guard static. The helicopter had launched but was holding east, weather-limited, waiting for a better approach picture. The clinic still could not speak directly to the cove. The person with the handheld radio was intermittent at best. The last relayed message was enough to make the room colder: fall injury, confusion, exposure, possible leg fracture, road cut off, no boat access.

Ruth looked at the case in Jane’s hand. “What is in the kit?”

He set it on the table. “Trauma supplies. Airway basics. Dressings. Splints. Thermal gear. Medications I would not use without a physician giving the order.”

The radio operator glanced over.

“You’re a doctor?” Ruth asked.

“No.”

“Medic?”

“No.”

“Then why do you carry that?”

Because Elias had believed empty space in an aircraft was a moral failure if one had ever seen the absence of supplies become the deciding fact. Because Mara had weighed every object and removed comfort before function. Because field clinics taught hierarchy until hierarchy went missing and then taught hands. Because after Elias died, the kit had remained, and Jane had not known whether carrying it was grief, habit, or refusal.

“Field experience,” he said.

Ruth held his gaze for a second. The answer did not satisfy her. It did, however, fit the documents she had asked him to bring.

“You do not provide patient care unless clinic directs it and I authorize it.”

Jane said nothing. He was at the airfield. The patient was not.

Ruth returned to the map.

The old strip. The beach. The helicopter waiting east of the weather. The road gone at Sisters Creek. Harbor refusing the head. Two local aircraft outside, one opened up and one trapped under maintenance. The black Kitfox cooling on the apron with rain moving along its wings.

Ruth did not pretend the choice was clean.

“I need eyes over the cove,” she said. “Not a rescue flight. Observation only.”

Jane looked at her.

“One observer. Wade goes with you. You identify wires, beach approach, wind at the cove, and patient location for Coast Guard. You do not land. You do not drop supplies. You do not make patient contact. Then you return here.”

Wade had already picked up his jacket.

Jane said, “Left brake went soft on taxi.”

Ruth looked at him sharply.

“Likely fitting,” he said. “Not relevant to an observation pass. Landing here is manageable. I would not take the cannery strip without another look.”

Wade moved toward the door. “I’ll check it before we roll.”

Ruth pointed to the medical kit. “That stays here.”

Jane’s hand closed once.

Ruth saw it. “Observation only. I’m not sending the implication of patient care in that airplane.”

That was logical. He hated it for being logical.

He left the kit on the table.

The second flight was shorter and worse.

Wade sat beside him with the portable radio strapped across his chest and one hand braced above the door, not from fear but because he understood that light aircraft were not furniture. At the hold short line he opened the door, leaned down into the rain, touched the left brake line fitting, and closed the door again.

“Fitting,” he said.

Jane nodded.

Then they were moving.

The Kitfox lifted into rain with less runway than Wade probably expected. St. Jude’s municipal fell away beneath them: runway, fuel pump, rescue building, flag, grounded aircraft, harbor below, head beyond shouldering cloud. Jane kept the climb shallow until the airspeed was honest, then turned north before the ridge could decide for him.

They crossed the road washout at five hundred feet. From above, Sisters Creek looked less like a creek than a place where the hillside had opened a vein. Brown water cut through gravel and alder, carrying sections of road shoulder toward the sea. A rescue truck waited on the near side with lights turning in the rain. Two figures stood beside it, made small by their own usefulness ending at the broken road.

Jane gave Wade the left side over the cove.

Mercy Cove appeared beneath the next band.

This pass was lower, slower, and wider, still within the clearance Ruth had given. The old utility line sagged from the pump house toward the cannery mast exactly where a helicopter would hate it. The standing figure had moved closer to the old strip and was waving both arms. The kneeling figure remained near the patient. The strip showed worse from this angle and then, as the rain shifted, better.

Wade transmitted. “Station, Wade. Confirm wire from pump house to cannery mast, north-south across inner approach. Beach hoist possible from seaward side only. Patient position fifteen yards west of cannery wall. No safe road access.”

Ruth answered. “Copy. Continue observation. Do not land.”

Jane held the aircraft in the edge of a turn. The patient’s tarp had shifted. A pale face, maybe. One boot angled wrong. The kneeling person looked up once as the aircraft passed and then bent back down.

On the second pass, the standing person ran.

Not far. From the cannery wall to the old strip, stumbling over wet grass and gravel, arms wide in the universal language of aircraft and desperation. He dragged something white behind him. At first Jane thought it was a tarp. Then the shape caught and lifted and he saw rigid edges: a sheet of old plastic roofing. The man pulled it off the middle of the strip and threw it toward the driftwood line. Beneath it was gravel, wet but intact, hidden from the earlier angle. Not clean. Not safe. Not permission. Longer than it had looked.

Wade leaned forward.

Jane had already seen it.

The west end remained soft. The east end was cut by runoff. The usable middle had just become a possibility.

“Station,” Wade transmitted. “Ground party cleared debris from the old strip. We have a possible short landing surface. Repeat possible, not confirmed.”

Ruth came back at once. “Negative landing. Continue observation.”

Jane kept the turn steady.

Below them, the standing man dropped to one knee.

At first Jane thought he had fallen from exhaustion. Then the man leaned forward and pressed both hands to his leg. The kneeling person by the patient looked up and waved once, not toward the aircraft this time, but toward the strip.

Wade stopped transmitting.

The Coast Guard helicopter came onto the frequency, broken by weather. “Station, Coast Guard. We are losing ceiling at the head. Holding east. Cannot commit to hover without a cleaner window.”

Ruth answered them. Jane did not hear the words clearly.

He was looking at the strip.

It would take a high approach and a short roll. The wind was wrong but not impossible. The runoff at the east end meant no go-around after a certain point. Driftwood on the seaward side, cannery wall inland, wet grass beyond, people too close until they understood. Weight acceptable with two aboard and fuel already burned down. Takeoff worse. Takeoff with a patient impossible unless the world improved, which it would not.

Landing would not evacuate anyone.

It would put two more people and an aircraft inside the problem.

It would also put hands on the patient while the weather still allowed hands to matter.

Jane looked at Wade.

Wade looked at the strip, then at the patient, then at the weather closing over the head.

No one said Ruth’s instruction. She had said it clearly enough.

Wade reached to the panel and turned the radio volume down, not off.

Jane said, “You know the cove.”

Wade nodded once.

That was the discussion.

Jane brought the nose around.

The approach did not feel heroic. It felt narrow and ugly and full of numbers that had to remain numbers. Airspeed. Gust spread. Sink rate. Surface. Escape point. The old strip held still below him, which meant nothing and had to mean enough. Wade braced one hand against the frame and kept his eyes on the ground.

“Deer track on the right,” Wade said.

Jane shifted left.

“Soft west. Don’t roll long.”

“I know.”

“Say it.”

“Soft west. No long roll.”

The wheels touched gravel with a wet slap, lifted once, touched again. Jane held the aircraft light as long as the surface would allow, then let it settle before the west end softened. The Kitfox rolled shorter than it should have and longer than he wanted. Gravel hissed under the tires. Wade said nothing. The aircraft slowed, slowed more, and stopped with prop wash flattening the wet grass ahead.

For half a second nobody moved.

Then Ruth’s voice came through the lowered radio, thin but unmistakable.

“Seven-three-four kilo, report.”

Wade did not look at the radio.

Jane pulled the mixture, killed the engine, and listened to the propeller stop.

The cove rushed in around the silence: rain on metal, surf beyond the hook of rock, a man shouting from the cannery wall, the small animal sound of someone in pain trying not to spend strength on it.

Wade opened his door. “Now we report.”

Jane climbed out into wet grass and gravel.

The standing man was on one knee near the edge of the strip, one hand pressed to his leg. The kneeling person by the patient was a woman in a soaked blue jacket, her hair plastered flat, one hand on the patient’s shoulder and the other holding a handheld radio that had gone dead or might as well have.

The patient lay on a tarp against the cannery wall.

Young man. Twenties, maybe. Pale. Eyes half open and not tracking. Rain had made every detail uncertain except the wrong angle of the leg, the slackness of his hands, and the absence of any useful warmth left in him.

Jane reached for the medical kit that was not there.

The absence moved through his body so sharply he almost looked back at the aircraft as if the kit might have followed him by habit.

It had not. Ruth had told him to leave it. He had obeyed.

For three seconds, obedience stood between him and the patient like a wall.

Then Wade was beside him with the aircraft survival pouch, two wool blankets from the baggage shelf, a roll of duct tape, and the small first-aid tin Jane kept in the cockpit because Elias had taught redundancy with the patience of a man describing gravity.

“Not your big kit,” Wade said.

“No.”

“Then small.”

Jane took the tin.

The contents were not enough. Gloves, gauze, compressed dressing, triangular bandage, small shears, tape, space blanket, two pairs of nitrile gloves, alcohol wipes, a pencil, safety pins, water purification tablets, an old packet of oral glucose. The sort of kit people carried to say they had one. Better than nothing. Worse than what he had left on Ruth’s table.

The woman in the blue jacket looked at him as he knelt. “Are you the medic?”

“No.”

The answer hurt her face.

“I have field experience. Clinic is on the radio. What’s his name?”

“Caleb.”

Jane leaned close enough that the patient might hear without needing to understand. “Caleb. I’m Jane. I’m going to check you.”

Caleb’s eyes moved but did not find him.

Wade had the portable radio out now, antenna up, body turned to shield it from rain. “Station, Wade. We are on the ground at Mercy Cove. Patient contact in progress. Aircraft intact. No injuries to us.”

The frequency erupted.

Ruth cut through it. “Say again.”

Wade repeated himself.

There was a silence so complete that even static seemed to step away from it.

Then Ruth said, “Put clinic with Jane.”

No shouting. Not yet. Shouting was for after the patient was moving.

Jane worked by voice and sequence. He gave the clinic what he could confirm, nothing more. Airway. Breathing. Pulse. Skin. Mental status. Obvious injury. Exposure. He said unknown when he did not know. He said likely when he was estimating. He kept Caleb warm, kept him still, protected him from rain as best the small kit allowed, and made the improvised supplies answer questions they had not been made for.

The clinic voice asked questions in order. Jane answered in order. Wade repeated what needed repeating and moved when Jane pointed, bringing driftwood, folded fabric, duct tape, and a length of aluminum conduit from the collapsed wall. Together they made an ugly splint, which was sometimes the only honest kind.

The man with the cut on his leg tried to stand and nearly fell.

“Sit,” Jane said.

The man looked at him blankly.

Wade said, “Aaron. Sit.”

Aaron sat.

The blue-jacket woman kept one hand on Caleb’s shoulder. She was shaking hard now that someone else had taken over the shape of action.

“You did well,” Jane said without looking away from the patient.

She began crying silently, which was inconvenient but not a problem.

The helicopter came twelve minutes later.

It arrived from the water side, low and loud, pushing rain outward in violent sheets. Jane stayed low over Caleb, one hand holding the improvised covering in place. Wade moved to the strip with the orange panel the standing man had been using and marked the wire hazard from the seaward side. He did not look like a man disobeying orders. He looked like a man extending an existing mistake until it became useful.

The Coast Guard did not land. They hoisted.

First Caleb. Then Aaron, who said he could wait and was ignored. The woman in blue remained because there was no room, then because she could walk once the road team found a foot route down from the west. When the helicopter lifted away, the cove seemed abruptly larger and less real.

Jane stood beside the Kitfox with rain running off his hair, gloves wet, shoes soaked through. The aircraft sat where he had put it, black against the strip, more vulnerable on the ground than it had ever looked in the air.

Wade came to stand beside him.

Neither of them spoke for a while.

Then Wade turned the radio volume back up.

Ruth’s voice was waiting.

“Wade.”

“Here.”

“Patient airborne?”

“Yes.”

“Aircraft?”

“Intact. Brake fitting still weeping.”

“Mr. Jane?”

Wade glanced at him. “Standing.”

“Return when safe,” Ruth said. “Not before.”

The return takeoff was worse than the landing.

It required moving debris, walking the strip, arguing with the west end, and waiting six minutes for a rain band to pass. Jane did not rush it. Disobedience did not make physics sentimental. Wade walked the usable section twice, boots sinking slightly near the wet end, and marked the softest patch with a strip of orange plastic roofing. They turned the Kitfox by hand. Jane checked the brake fitting again and wiped rain off the windshield with his sleeve.

Before climbing in, he looked once toward the cannery wall.

The blue-jacket woman sat beneath the overhang with both arms around herself. The dead handheld radio lay beside her. She raised one hand. Not waving this time. Just acknowledgment.

Jane lifted his hand once and got into the aircraft.

The Kitfox left the cove with no elegance. It bumped, gathered, skipped once over gravel, then lifted into rain with the stall horn thinking about speaking and deciding not to. Wade did not breathe until they cleared the hook of rock.

“That was close,” Wade said.

“Yes.”

“Don’t make close a habit.”

Jane kept the nose down until the airspeed was honest. “I don’t.”

St. Jude’s municipal came back in a darker rain. Jane landed with the soft brake in mind and did not need it much. He rolled clear, taxied to the apron, and shut down in front of the rescue building because Ruth was already outside.

She stood in the rain without her hood, arms at her sides, the framed authority of the building behind her and the island standard cracking above the door. Behind her, through lit windows, people moved around the map wall and radio desk, turning the emergency into reports, calls, equipment checks, and the first version of what had happened.

Wade climbed out first.

Ruth did not look at Jane yet. “Report.”

Wade gave it to her in order. Landing surface. Patient status on arrival. Care given under clinic direction. Helicopter hoist. Aircraft condition. Brake fitting. Takeoff delay. Return flight.

Ruth listened without interrupting. Rain ran off the ends of her hair and darkened the collar of her jacket. When Wade finished, she looked at Jane.

“You accepted one pass with one observer,” she said.

“Yes.”

“You landed.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“Ground party exposed a longer strip. Aaron went down. Coast Guard reported loss of ceiling. From the second pass, the landing was possible. Not clean. Possible.”

“Possible is not authorization.”

“No.”

“Did you believe delay would materially worsen the patient’s outcome?”

Jane did not answer quickly. The question deserved its own weight.

“Yes.”

“Based on what?”

“Exposure. Altered mental status. Mechanism. No road access. Coast Guard losing the window. The person with him had a dead radio and no equipment visible from the pass.”

Ruth held his answer in the rain for a moment.

“That is the answer you put in the report,” she said.

“Yes.”

She held out her hand. “Folder.”

Jane handed it to her.

“This is not punishment,” Ruth said. “It is documentation. Before today, you were a civilian pilot I had exchanged emails with. Then I put one of my people in your aircraft and gave you a task inside my incident. You changed the task. Maybe correctly. Maybe not. Either way, I now have to account for you.”

Jane said nothing.

“If your documents fail review, that becomes its own problem. If your aircraft fails Wade’s inspection, that becomes its own problem. If your account of your medical experience does not match what the clinic heard on the radio, that becomes its own problem.”

“Yes.”

“And if all of it holds,” Ruth said, “that does not make you cleared. It makes you something I have to decide how to use without letting urgency write policy for me.”

Jane looked at her then. She was not being cruel. She was building a fence around a fire and deciding whether the fire was worth keeping.

“I understand,” he said.

“I don’t think you do yet. But you understood enough in the cove to say possible instead of safe. That matters.”

The rain struck the apron hard enough to bounce.

Ruth looked toward the hangars. “You rented hangar two.”

“Yes.”

“You rented storage.”

“I occupy space.”

For a moment, something almost changed in her face. It did not become humor.

“Tonight,” she said. “Because the shelter bunks are full, because half my people are still wet, and because I do not have a spare person to drive you around the harbor looking for a room you will refuse. Tomorrow we discuss what that arrangement violates.”

Wade said, “It violates several things.”

“I know,” Ruth said.

She turned back toward the building with his folder under her arm.

Wade stayed beside the aircraft. Together, without discussing it, he and Jane rolled the Kitfox into hangar two. The tires crossed the threshold with a small wet hiss. Inside, the black wings took what little light the hangar offered and kept it. Jane secured the aircraft properly this time: chocks, straps, controls, pitot cover, door latch, cowling seam wiped dry with a rag from his pocket.

Wade watched the order of it.

“You always put the plane to bed before yourself?”

“Yes.”

“That a rule or a symptom?”

Jane checked the left brake line again. The fitting left a faint sheen on his fingertip.

“Yes,” he said.

Wade accepted that too.

When he left, the hangar became large around the aircraft. Rain struck the roof in uneven lines. Somewhere above, a gull moved with dry clicking steps over the metal. The rescue building glowed across the apron. Voices occasionally rose when the door opened, then disappeared into weather.

Jane brought in the duffel, the tool case, the books, the oilcloth-wrapped notebook that had belonged to Elias. He did not unpack much. He did not have enough claim on the place to spread himself through it. Cot behind the partition where the floor looked dry. Tool case under the bench. Folder gone with Ruth. Medical kit still in the rescue building, which bothered him more than he wanted to show anyone, though no one was there to see.

Last, he stood beside the Kitfox and placed one hand on the wing strut.

The metal was cold now. Only metal.

“You did fine,” he said.

The words sounded foolish and necessary. He had said them to the aircraft before, in other places, after other weather. Elias had heard him once and said nothing for almost a full minute before remarking that praise given to machines was harmless if maintenance followed. Mara would have objected to the category of harmless. She objected to categories in general when they were used lazily.

Jane took his hand away.

Outside, the island standard snapped loose from its pole and opened fully in the field lights: dark blue, white border, St. Jude with his raised staff or weapon or whatever the island had agreed to call the thing so it could remain respectable. The saint faced the weather as if weather were an argument he had heard before and intended to win by endurance alone.

Jane watched the flag fold back into itself.

Then he lay down on the cot in his clothes, barefoot under a blanket that smelled faintly of canvas and fuel, while rain moved over the hangar roof and the black wing above him held the last of the light.
