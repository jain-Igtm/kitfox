# Chapter One — St. Jude’s Head

Ten minutes after Jane arrived on St. Jude’s Head, the rain stopped pretending to fall from the sky.

It moved sideways now, fine and cold, driven off the ridge above the airfield in separate sheets he could watch crossing the ground. One passed over the cut grass beyond the runway, darkened the fuel shed, blurred the municipal truck parked beside the pump, and struck the left wing of the Kitfox with a sound too soft to be called impact. The black fabric did not shine under it. The aircraft had been black before the rain, but under this weather it became nearly depthless, a hard absence held together by struts, rivets, patched seams, and the pale registration along the tail.

Jane stood beside the cowling with one hand laid flat on the metal until the engine warmth was gone.

There was no use in touching it longer. He knew the oil temperature had been right when he shut down. He knew the left brake had softened near the end of taxi. He knew the gust off the quarry had been worse than the advisory suggested and still not as bad as the place could probably manage. The machine had crossed the water, found the runway, obeyed the last correction. It deserved to be left in a state where the next request would not be an insult.

He removed his hand.

The island did not introduce itself. It gave him pieces.

A narrow runway laid on a bench of cut ground above the harbor. Three hangars repaired by three different eras of thrift. A fuel pump in a small fenced square. A low search-and-rescue building with antennas fixed to the roof and a sign whose blue letters had lifted at the corners: ST. JUDE’S HEAD SEARCH AND RESCUE. Below, where fog broke open and closed again, he could see the harbor: metal roofs, lines of masts, ferry pilings, a long gray breakwater, and water striking rock with the routine violence of something that had never been negotiated with successfully.

Beside the rescue building flew the island standard.

It was too formal for the weather and too severe for a flag at a municipal airstrip. Dark blue field, white border, the figure of St. Jude in an old style at the center. Robe. Narrow face. One arm raised with a staff or weapon or some local distinction between the two. The halo, seen at a distance through rain, had the wrong geometry. It looked less like holiness than a mark used by an army that had run out of patience.

The same figure appeared on the door of the municipal truck, on the rescue building window, and on the tails of two local aircraft tied down near the first hangar. One was a weather-beaten Super Cub with patched fabric and a red spinner. The other was a Cessna on wheels, floats stored beneath its wing on a low rack. The saint watched from both with identical severity.

The Kitfox carried no such mark.

Jane looked at the flag long enough to identify the question it would become later, then turned back to the aircraft.

He set the chocks deeper. The apron had a shallow inward grade and a skin of water over algae near the edge of the concrete. He crouched and adjusted the first chock with the heel of his hand. The gravel was cold under his feet. He had shoes in the aircraft. He had packed them because shoes were a condition of many rooms, not because they improved most situations. The ground told him what it was doing. Shoes tended to interrupt useful conversations.

Behind him, the rescue building door opened.

He did not turn at once. He looped the left tie-down strap through the ring, drew it back on itself, tightened, checked the angle, and only then looked over his shoulder.

A woman stood at the edge of the apron in a dark orange waterproof jacket faded at the shoulders. Her hood hung down her back despite the rain. Her hair was short, wet, and mostly gray at the temples. Nothing about her was arranged for warmth. She looked first at the aircraft, then at the straps, the chocks, his hands, his bare feet, and last of all his face.

He knew her from twelve emails and one voicemail he had not answered because there had been no efficient answer to give.

Ruth Calder.

In writing, she used complete sentences, few adjectives, and no exclamation marks. She had replied to his first message after thirty-one hours. Mr. Jane, St. Jude’s Head Search and Rescue is not hiring pilots. We are reviewing volunteer support for ground, logistics, radio, and maintenance only. If you intend to present yourself in person, bring aircraft registration, maintenance logs, proof of insurance, pilot documentation, medical references, and identification. Do not construe this as acceptance.

Jane had not construed it as acceptance. He had construed it as a door that was not locked.

“You’re early,” Ruth said.

“The weather window was earlier.”

“Your last email said tomorrow.”

“My last email said I would not arrive later than tomorrow.”

Ruth looked at the Kitfox again. “That is the kind of sentence people write when they expect to be disliked in advance.”

“It was precise.”

“Those are not opposites.”

A man stood under the eave of the rescue building, holding a mug with both hands. He wore a cap pulled low and a fleece vest over a dark thermal shirt. Broad through the shoulders, beard gone gray in uneven patches, eyes narrowed against weather more from habit than necessity. Jane recognized his voice from the radio advisory during approach: calm through static, giving wind and traffic information with the informal authority of someone the field had failed to replace with equipment.

Wade.

He had sent the hangar invoice. He had also added, in a separate line beneath the payment instructions: Door binds left. Lift before rolling. Don’t force it or I’ll know.

Jane had inspected the door that morning by ferry and found the warning accurate.

Wade lifted the mug once. Jane gave the smallest possible nod. It was accepted as currency and not discussed.

“You made decent time from Yakutat,” Ruth said.

“The tailwind helped until the quarry.”

“The quarry helps no one.”

“No.”

The radio inside the rescue building cracked loudly enough for the sound to pass through the open door. Ruth turned her head half an inch, listened to a clipped voice buried in static, and then looked back at him with less patience available than before.

That was when he understood that he had not arrived at the beginning of a conversation. He had arrived in the middle of a problem.

The evidence had been on the airfield before she spoke: the Super Cub tied down wrong for ready use, cowling open, one access panel removed; the Cessna blocked by float hardware and a maintenance stand; two trucks gone from the gravel lot beside the rescue building; an orange litter already laid out beneath the eave, rain beading on its straps; Wade on the advisory radio instead of in the hangar; Ruth meeting him herself instead of sending someone bored.

Something was active. Not complete. Not controlled.

Jane finished the second strap. “Aircraft unavailable?”

Ruth’s attention returned fully to him.

He nodded toward the Super Cub without looking at it again. “Cowling open. No covers staged. Tie-downs still crossed. It wasn’t parked after a normal flight.”

Wade came a little farther out from under the eave.

“Prop strike two days ago,” Ruth said. “Minor if the word is being used by someone with money.”

“Engine inspection not complete.”

“No.”

“The Cessna?”

“Waiting on a part from Juneau.”

“And the helicopter?”

Ruth’s face did not change, but the air around the question did. “Coast Guard is weather-limited north of the head. They’ll launch if it clears enough to make the approach worth the risk.”

“It won’t clear north first,” Jane said.

Wade looked toward the ridge. “No, it won’t.”

Ruth looked between them. “You two want to become annoying together later?”

Jane tightened the tail line and tested it with a short pull. “What happened?”

For a moment she did not answer. That was reasonable. He was not yet anyone entitled to the answer. He was a person who had written emails, attached logs, paid for three months of hangar space, arrived a day early in a black aircraft, and stood barefoot on her apron beneath a weather system she already had reason to hate.

Then the radio spoke again.

“Station, Overlook Two. Still no visual on north beach. Repeater intermittent. We’re losing the bottom of the cut.”

Ruth turned toward the building.

“Black Friar Rock,” Jane said.

She stopped.

He looked past her toward the harbor, though the harbor was no longer visible. Fog had taken it back. “You’re searching north of Apostle Cut.”

No one moved.

Wade lowered his mug.

Ruth said, “Why?”

“I heard partial traffic on one-two-two point nine before I switched to advisory. You were stepping on each other.”

“That doesn’t tell you Black Friar.”

“No.”

He looked down at the tail line, then at the map he had never seen inside the building but had studied before coming. The island had occupied him for ten days: charts, accident reports, ferry notices, local photographs, fishing forums, weather archives, search summaries, a newspaper article about a man who had survived eighteen hours in a kelp bed by tying himself to his overturned skiff. The names had become positions. The positions had become wind.

“I saw an orange panel east of Black Friar on approach,” he said. “Not on the rock. Inside of it. Low. It appeared twice between rain bands and then I lost it in spray. I called traffic with a possible visual but I don’t know who copied.”

Ruth looked at Wade.

Wade said, “I got something like possible orange, broken, from an inbound experimental. Didn’t get location before he switched. Thought it was airport traffic garbage.”

“Kitfox,” Jane said.

Wade looked at him.

“It’s a Kitfox.”

Ruth stepped closer. “Why didn’t you transmit again?”

“I was low, turning final, and your channel was occupied. Then I landed.”

“And tied down.”

“Yes.”

“Before telling me you saw the thing we are trying to find.”

“I am telling you now.”

Her eyes became very still.

He understood the anger. It was structurally sound. He had placed the aircraft first because the aircraft, unsecured, could become a second emergency, and because he had not yet known whether the orange panel was a tarp, a buoy, a fuel jug, a survival panel, or the island offering one of many stupid colors to rain. Still, the anger was sound. Delay required accounting.

“Time from sighting to touchdown was six minutes,” he said. “Time from touchdown to now is ten. Tide rising. Wind west-southwest, stronger over the cut than reported here. If the panel was attached to a skiff, it will not still be where I saw it. If it was on shore, it might.”

Ruth turned toward the open door and raised her voice. “Ana, mark Black Friar east inside. Possible orange panel from inbound aircraft, time minus sixteen.”

Inside, someone answered, “Copy.”

Wade had already set his mug down.

Ruth looked at Jane again. “Plane secure?”

“For now.”

“For now is a bad phrase near aircraft.”

“It is the honest one.”

“Hangar,” Wade said.

He rolled the door of hangar two open. It stuck on the left track and then gave under the lift of his shoulder. Jane pushed from the left strut while Wade took the right. Pride, weighed against wet concrete and wind, produced poor decisions; he let the man help. The Kitfox crossed the threshold slowly, tires hissing over the wet lip where gravel met concrete. Inside, the aircraft seemed larger, the black wing gathering the dim light and holding it close.

Jane chocked it again. He checked the brake line by sight and touch, not because it would repair itself, but because exact knowledge made delay tolerable. The pedal softness was likely at the fitting. Not catastrophic. Not ignorable.

“Soft brake?” Wade asked.

“Yes.”

“Fluid?”

“Yes.”

“Tools?”

Jane looked once at the hard case.

Wade nodded, as though the answer had been complete.

Against the north wall stood a warped workbench, a shelf, two coils of rope, a crate of metal fittings, and a calendar from a charter service whose photograph showed a bright boat under blue sky that probably had not belonged to St. Jude’s Head even when the calendar was new. Behind a plywood partition lay enough dry floor for a cot if the cot were placed diagonally. The roof leaked near the back corner, but not there. He had confirmed that during the morning ferry trip by sitting on the concrete for seventeen minutes and watching the water choose its routes.

“You’re not sleeping here,” Ruth said from the doorway.

“I paid three months.”

“You rented storage.”

“I am storing myself.”

Wade did not laugh. He looked toward the rescue building, where the radio sounded again.

Ruth followed the sound with her eyes. “We’ll argue about your living arrangements after I know whether I’m using you or throwing you off the island.”

Jane looked at her.

It was the first honest thing anyone had said to him on St. Jude’s Head.

He took the plastic-wrapped folder from the baggage compartment. He removed his duffel and hard tool case, set them under the wing, and left the medical kit inside. It was not hidden, exactly. Hiding required a relationship with discovery. He simply did not introduce it.

Ruth noticed the case he did not remove.

Wade noticed Ruth noticing.

No one had time to make it a subject.

The rescue building was warmer than the hangar and less orderly than its exterior had promised. Heat came from a wall unit below one window and a portable heater aimed at a rack of wet gloves. Maps covered the largest wall. Not decorative maps. Working maps, layered with grease-pencil marks, tide windows, cliff access points, radio shadows, old slide zones, informal names, wreck sites, and red circles around places whose danger was apparently too well known to need a label. A second wall held helmets, harnesses, flotation suits, throw bags, medical packs, spare boots, radios in chargers, and a shelf marked AIR OPS with headsets, signal panels, spare batteries, and two handheld GPS units old enough to have a temperament.

Above the radio desk hung the St. Jude standard in a frame.

Indoors, the saint looked less like a symbol and more like a verdict someone had learned to respect. The raised object in his hand was probably a staff. Probably. The local artists had not helped the question.

Three people worked inside. A woman with dark hair tied back stood at the radio desk with a pencil between her fingers. An older man in orange rain gear leaned over the north coast map, one hand braced on the wall as if holding the island still. A younger volunteer sat on a bench threading batteries into a waterproof case with hands that shook slightly from cold or adrenaline. All of them looked at Jane, then at his feet, then at Ruth. Not long enough to be rude. Long enough to prove rudeness had been considered.

“Shoes in the building,” Ruth said.

“During calls, gear handling, medical work, fuel, metal work, and any floor with blood or glass.”

The woman at the radio looked down at her clipboard to hide something.

Wade said, “He came with categories.”

Ruth held Jane there with her eyes. “You negotiate every rule before entering a room?”

“Only the broad ones.”

“This is a call.”

Jane put on his shoes.

They were in the side pocket of his duffel, still dry. The socks were not as dry as he would have liked. The floor became less useful immediately.

Ruth pointed to the map. “Show me.”

He crossed to the wall.

Apostle Cut was marked in red, with Black Friar Rock just west of the narrowest channel. Someone had drawn three possible drift lines in grease pencil. Two went too far north. One ended in a question mark near kelp beds that would be almost invisible in the chop. Jane took the pencil Ruth handed him and waited one second, long enough to be sure she truly meant him to mark her map.

Then he drew a small X inside the eastern lee of Black Friar, closer to shore than the existing search circle.

“Here. It flashed in a gap between bands. If it was a panel, it was low and wet. If it was a survival tarp, it was partly wrapped. If it was a boat surface, it was wrong-side up.”

The older man at the map exhaled through his nose. “That’s inside the teeth.”

“Yes.”

“Boat team can’t enter that clean in this water.”

“No.”

Ruth said, “Could you see people?”

“No.”

“Smoke?”

“No.”

“Movement?”

“Not reliable.”

“Meaning?”

“Something changed shape between views. It may have been fabric moving. It may have been water.”

“Range from aircraft?”

“Maybe nine hundred feet lateral. I was not measuring it. I was avoiding your quarry.”

Wade looked at the map, then at him. “You came in lower than most.”

“The ceiling asked.”

“The ceiling asks a lot of things here.”

“I declined several.”

Ruth studied the X. Her face had not softened. It had become more efficient.

“Ana,” she said.

The woman at the radio lifted her pencil.

“Update marine. Possible visual inside Black Friar, east lee, time now minus eighteen. Tell them not to enter the teeth unless harbor lead confirms a safe line. Ask Overlook Two if they can shift west ten degrees and glass that pocket when the band lifts.”

Ana repeated it cleanly into the radio.

Static answered first. Then a voice from the boat, strained by engine noise and water. “Copy possible visual. We cannot make inside approach from current position. Standing off.”

The older man at the map said, “They’ll lose it if they stand off.”

“They’ll lose their hull if they don’t,” Ruth said.

Jane looked at the contour lines behind Black Friar. The cliff above the pocket had a narrow saddle leading down from the old cannery road. On the map it looked passable. On satellite it had looked deceptive. The slope carried water. Rockfall. Brush. No clean extraction if someone was injured. But a visual from above might confirm whether the orange was worth risking a boat.

“You have a path from the quarry road,” he said.

Ruth looked at him.

He pointed without touching the map. “Not to the beach. To the overlook above the pocket. Here. It won’t be fast. It may be faster than waiting for marine access.”

The older man shook his head. “Old goat track. Bad in rain.”

“How bad?” Jane asked.

“Bad.”

“That’s not a unit.”

The man stared at him.

Jane looked back. He did not mean it as insolence. Most people received precision as insult when stress had already lowered their tolerance.

Ruth said, “Rope team can make that ridge in forty if they leave now.”

“Thirty-five,” the older man said. “If Cole’s with them.”

“Cole is on the boat.”

“Then forty-five.”

Ana looked up from the radio. “Overlook Two says no visual. Band is too low. They’re getting fogged out.”

Ruth’s jaw moved once.

Jane listened to the wind at the building seams, the radio hiss, the rain against the windows, the pattern of people choosing among bad tools. The room reminded him of clinics where the generator failed mid-procedure, where the question was never what should be done in a clean world, but what could be done before the dirty one became worse.

Mara had taught him that numbers were not answers. Elias had taught him that neither was authority. Both had taught him, without saying so, that panic often disguised itself as waiting for better conditions.

“Your portable repeater works?” Jane asked.

Wade looked at the AIR OPS shelf.

Ruth said, “Sometimes.”

“Battery?”

“Charged.”

“Weight?”

“Twelve pounds with case,” Wade said.

“Signal panel?”

“Orange.”

Jane looked at the map again. “If the ridge team takes the repeater, marine can hold farther off and still coordinate with them. If the orange is fabric, they can mark it from above. If there are people below and no boat approach, you’ll need Coast Guard when the window opens.”

Ruth watched him for a long second. “You just arrived.”

“Yes.”

“You’re not on my team.”

“No.”

“You’re not cleared for anything.”

“No.”

“And you are telling me my deployment.”

“I am telling you the repeater is more useful on the ridge than on that shelf.”

Wade set down his mug for the second time, which Jane had already learned meant he was done pretending not to move.

“He’s right,” Wade said.

Ruth did not thank either of them. “Pack the repeater. Jonah, get Elise and Morgan off standby. Ridge team goes in ten. Ana, tell harbor we’re putting eyes above the pocket. Tell clinic to stay warm but don’t roll down yet.”

The room changed shape around the decision.

No one welcomed Jane into it. That was the first thing he liked about the place. They did not have time to decide whether he belonged. They had only enough time to use the part of his statement that survived contact with the map.

Wade took a waterproof case from the AIR OPS shelf and opened it on the table. The younger volunteer brought batteries. One of them was wrong for the unit. Jane saw it before Wade touched it.

“Not that one,” he said.

The volunteer froze.

Wade looked into the case. “Why?”

“Different connector. Same casing.”

Wade picked it up, turned it once, and saw the notch. His mouth tightened. “Who stored this here?”

No one answered.

Ruth was on the radio and did not turn, but Jane felt her hear it.

He removed the correct battery from the second row. “This one.”

Wade took it from him and slotted it into the unit. The repeater woke with a green light and a short tone.

“Useful,” Wade said.

Jane did not answer. Useful was not praise. Useful was a condition, sometimes safe and sometimes not.

The ridge team arrived in pieces: two people from the back room, one from outside with rain already running off his jacket, one older woman who moved slowly until she put on a pack and then seemed to become a different animal. Helmets. Harnesses. Waterproof case. Throw bag. Medical pack. Signal panel. They checked each other without tenderness. Clips, straps, radios, batteries, knots. The St. Jude patch appeared on every sleeve, severe and identical, the island’s armed saint duplicated on bodies preparing to go into weather.

Jane stood out of the way until Wade pointed at the log table.

“Write.”

Jane sat.

The log sheet was already ruled by hand. Time, station, message, action. He wrote the active time, the last known position, the reported visual, the revised search area. His handwriting became small and even. When Ana repeated radio traffic, he recorded it without embellishment. When words vanished under static, he wrote unclear. Elias had once taken a clipboard from him in a clinic after he guessed at a number from memory. If it is not observed, it is not recorded. If it is guessed, it is labeled. Guessing honestly is not the same as knowing.

The room worked.

Not smoothly. Smoothness belonged to places with money. The room worked the way a repair worked: with pressure, adjustment, reused parts, competent irritation. Ruth held the center. Wade moved between gear and radio and door. Ana translated static into record. The ridge team left with the repeater case between two packs. The boat team held off Black Friar and waited in water that did not care what anyone needed.

Jane wrote.

At 15:47, ridge team departed airfield.

At 15:52, marine holding outside Black Friar, unsafe inside approach.

At 16:03, Overlook Two lost visual, descending.

At 16:11, ridge team reached quarry road gate.

At 16:22, portable repeater active, signal improved.

At 16:29, ridge team visual on orange object.

The room stopped.

Ana held one hand to the headset. “Repeat.”

Static. Rain. A voice breathing hard. “Orange panel confirmed. Looks attached to overturned skiff. Possible person on rock shelf above it. Hard to see. One person. Maybe two. No movement confirmed.”

Ruth closed her eyes for less than a second.

When she opened them, she was already pointing. “Marine stays off. Ridge team does not descend without anchor. Coast Guard gets this now. Tell them we have possible survivors on rock shelf east of Black Friar, no marine access, rope access possible but slow, weather window uncertain.”

Jane wrote until the pencil point cracked. He turned it and kept writing with the broken side.

The next twenty minutes did not belong to him.

They belonged to distances, knots, engine noise, ceilings, wind, fuel, people on radios trying to make their version of the weather match everyone else’s. They belonged to the ridge team lowering one member far enough to confirm two survivors, one responsive, one not clearly responsive. They belonged to a Coast Guard pilot asking for cloud base and crosswind and terrain clearance. They belonged to Ruth saying no when someone wanted to move faster than anchors allowed. They belonged to Wade standing in the doorway with rain hitting his boots, listening as if he could hear rope strain from the building.

Jane wrote.

Once, Ruth came behind him and read over his shoulder. She did not say good. She took the completed page, placed a fresh one beside his hand, and returned to the map.

That was better.

At 17:08, the Coast Guard launched.

At 17:26, ridge team reached survivor one.

At 17:39, survivor one confirmed breathing, hypothermic, arm injury, able to answer name.

At 17:44, survivor two confirmed breathing, unconscious or semi-conscious, head injury suspected.

At 18:02, helicopter established visual west of Apostle Cut.

At 18:11, hoist began.

No one in the room celebrated when the first survivor came off the rock. Celebration required spare capacity. They marked it, repeated it, moved to the second. Jane’s wrist hurt. His shoulders had become stiff from stillness. His feet, trapped in shoes, were cold in a less informative way than before.

At 18:34, survivor two aboard helicopter.

At 18:39, helicopter departing to clinic pad.

At 18:42, ridge team holding position for extraction by trail.

At 18:51, marine released from Black Friar.

Only then did Ruth step back from the map.

The room loosened by degrees, like a hand unclenching one finger at a time. Someone sat on the floor. Someone swore softly at a radio battery. Ana removed her headset and pressed both palms over her ears. Wade went to the heater, took a glove from the rack, realized it was not his, and put it back with unusual care.

Jane placed the final log sheet on the stack and aligned the corners.

Ruth came to the table.

Her jacket had darkened at the shoulders, though she had not gone outside since the ridge team left. Her face looked older in the yellow light. Not tired exactly. Reduced to essentials.

She read the top sheet, then the second, then the page where he had marked the first orange-panel report. She did not rush. People who rushed records after an incident either feared what they would find or expected the paper to forgive them. Ruth did neither.

“You wrote possible,” she said.

“It was possible.”

“You could have said confirmed.”

“No.”

“Could have made yourself look better.”

“That would make the record worse.”

Wade looked over from the heater.

Ruth set the pages down. “Your email said you had radio experience.”

“Yes.”

“It did not say you were tolerable at a log table.”

“I didn’t know that was the standard.”

“It is today.”

The words were not warm. They were not meant to be. They did something more useful than warmth: they established a fact in the room.

Jane nodded once.

Ruth looked down at his shoes. “You can take those off now.”

He did.

Ana made a quiet sound from the radio desk that might have been disbelief, or laughter, or the failure to have energy for either.

Without shoes, the floor returned: grit, damp seams, the slight vibration of the heater, the cool track of water someone had brought in from outside. Jane’s mind settled half an inch closer to his body.

Ruth picked up the plastic-wrapped folder he had left near the map table. “Office.”

The office was narrow, glass-walled, and fighting moisture badly. The dehumidifier hummed in the corner with the exhausted optimism of an undersized machine. Binders stood along one shelf, labels curling at the edges. On the windowsill sat a cracked radio battery, a model floatplane, and a mug printed with the St. Jude figure in red. In red, the saint looked less like a patron of lost causes than someone sent to audit them.

Jane sat because she pointed to the chair.

The chair was uncomfortable in a deliberate way. Its front edge pressed beneath the thigh. Its back angle discouraged anything like rest. Ruth’s chair looked no better. That improved the office’s moral standing.

She unwrapped the folder carefully.

That mattered. People who treated documents roughly usually believed documents were paper. They were not. They were tools for moving permission, liability, and blame from one place to another. A bent page could become a question. A missing signature could become an excuse. A date could become a locked door.

She began with the aircraft registration, then insurance, then the maintenance logs. Her eyes moved slowly through the modification list: extended gear, larger tires, vortex generators, fuel system notes, baggage compartment alteration, avionics work, transponder inspection, fabric repair, propeller log. Wade stood just inside the doorway and looked out through the glass, which let him read the reflection.

“Black,” Ruth said.

Jane waited.

“You painted a working aircraft black.”

“Yes.”

“Over water.”

“It isn’t over water when it’s being painted.”

Wade made a small sound that did not quite become a laugh.

Ruth looked up. “Aircraft used around search operations are sometimes searched for.”

“Yes.”

“You see the issue.”

“I see an issue.”

“Why black?”

He could have given the useful answer. It showed certain leaks. It made repairs visible in a particular way. It was easy to touch up without caring about beauty. All of that was true enough to be dishonest.

He thought of the Kitfox in primer under shop lights, its old color stripped away in dull curls, the fabric patched where weather and time had made claims. Elias had been alive then and already moving carefully when he thought Jane was not looking. The black had gone on in two coats. After the first, the aircraft stopped looking cheerful. After the second, it stopped looking like it belonged to anyone who expected the world to be gentle with it.

“It suited the plane,” Jane said.

Ruth accepted that more readily than she would have accepted an explanation.

Then she reached the pilot documents.

The office narrowed.

Jane looked at the peeling laminate on the desk instead of her face. The papers were good enough for a first glance. Some of them would survive a phone call. Some would survive two if the person on the other end was lazy, busy, or sympathetic. Some were true in a way that depended on how much weight one placed on supervision, experience, jurisdiction, and the difference between ability and permission. He had not made them to fool careful people. He had made them because careless people controlled many doors, and sometimes careful people were tired.

Ruth read one page twice. Then another.

“Which of these survives if I call Anchorage?” she asked.

“Aircraft documents.”

“And the rest?”

“Depends who answers.”

Wade stopped looking out the glass.

Ruth did not move for several seconds. “That is not the answer of someone who misunderstands the question.”

“No.”

“Your emails were signed J. Jane.”

“Yes.”

“Jane is your last name.”

“Yes.”

She looked again at the documents. “You prefer it?”

“I answer to it.”

“That was not what I asked.”

“No.”

She let that stand. It was the closest thing to mercy she had offered so far.

The medical references came next. Overseas clinics. Field organizations with inconsistent letterheads. Names Ruth would not know and a few she might if she knew where to look. Elias’s letter was there too, scanned from clinic stationery, his handwriting narrow and abrupt where he had corrected a date in the margin. Jane had not read it since the funeral. It described ability without admiration, responsibility without softness, limits observed, procedures assisted, errors corrected, conditions tolerated. Elias alive in the most Elias way possible: precise, withholding, useful after departure.

Ruth read it to the end.

“Elias Venn,” she said.

“Yes.”

“Doctor.”

“Yes.”

“Guardian.”

“Yes.”

“Dead.”

The word did not become gentler because it was accurate.

“Yes.”

“When?”

“Six weeks.”

Wade looked down.

Outside the office, someone in the main room set down a cup too hard, then steadied it with a hand. Jane felt the room beyond the glass rearrange around the new information. Not gossip first. Awareness first. Fact enters, person re-sorted, eyes redirected too late.

“Sorry,” Ruth said.

Jane nodded because nodding cost less than speech.

She placed Elias’s letter beside the pilot documents, not on top of them. The separation was deliberate.

“You are not medical staff here.”

“No.”

“You do not present yourself as medical staff.”

“No.”

“If someone is hurt, you tell my medic what you know and do what the lead tells you.”

“What if the lead is wrong?”

Wade sighed, very quietly.

Ruth’s face remained still. “Then you will have encountered one of civilization’s older design flaws. You will still not make it worse by needing to be important.”

Jane looked at her then.

The sentence found him more accurately than he wanted.

“I don’t need to be important,” he said.

“No,” Ruth said. “I read your emails. You need to be necessary. That is worse.”

The office held the words without softening them.

Outside, the framed St. Jude standard hung above the radio desk, visible through the glass. The saint’s raised arm cut the dark blue field at a severe angle. Jane thought of the same figure on the aircraft outside. The local planes wore him as if belonging could be painted in miniature near a door. The Kitfox, black and unmarked in hangar two, remained itself.

Ruth closed the folder.

“Here is what is true,” she said. “You are not cleared to fly for St. Jude’s Head Search and Rescue.”

“No.”

“You are not on my roster.”

“No.”

“Your paperwork has problems I do not have the afternoon to finish disliking.”

“Yes.”

“You heard something, saw something, recorded accurately, improved a deployment, and did not touch a radio until told.”

“Yes.”

“You also delayed reporting a possible visual because you were securing your aircraft.”

“Yes.”

“Do you understand why that makes me angry?”

“Yes.”

“Do you understand why I cannot entirely call it wrong?”

Jane did not answer immediately.

The question deserved better than convenience.

“Yes,” he said.

Ruth leaned back. The chair complained under her the same way it had under him.

“Ground probation,” she said. “Radio log. Gear inventory. Maintenance under Wade. Map work. Weather observation. You do not self-dispatch. You do not transport patients. You do not act as medical personnel. You do not fly for this organization.”

The last sentence had edges around it.

Jane heard the sentence she did not say: yet.

Or perhaps he invented it because invention was easier than hunger.

“How long?” he asked.

“Until I decide whether you are a tool or a hazard.”

“Those can overlap.”

“I know. I run SAR.”

Wade said from the doorway, “Brake line first thing tomorrow.”

Jane looked at him.

Wade looked back. “If you’re going to be a hazard, I prefer you stop evenly.”

Ruth stood. “Seven tomorrow. Earlier if the ridge team needs turnaround support. You can use hangar two tonight.”

Jane did not move.

She noticed. “Do not make me sentimental about it. The shelter beds are full because half my people are still out, and I don’t have time to send Wade down the harbor road to find you a room you won’t use.”

“I wasn’t going to thank you.”

“Good.”

He gathered the folder. His hands were steady. He had not known they might not be until they were.

In the main room, the operation had become aftermath. Ana was rewriting the final log in ink. Someone was on the phone with the clinic. A volunteer carried wet rope toward the gear room. The older man at the map had removed his jacket and stood in shirtsleeves, looking at the red X near Black Friar as if it had personally insulted him by being correct.

Jane returned to the hangar with his duffel, tool case, and folder.

Evening had arrived while the room was working. The airfield lights burned low through mist. Water dripped from roof edges, struts, cables, gutters. The whole field ticked and clicked in the cold. Down the slope, harbor lights showed as yellow stains behind fog. Somewhere below, two survivors were being warmed, cut out of wet clothing, examined, and turned from a problem into people again. Somewhere on the ridge, a team was still walking home in rain.

Hangar two received him without opinion.

He set the folder on the workbench, disliked how close it was to the solvent can, moved the solvent can to the far shelf, then moved the folder again because the shelf above it dripped once every thirty-six seconds. The cot went behind the plywood partition where the floor stayed dry. Sleeping bag. One folded blanket. Tool case under the bench. Spare parts in a plastic bin. Books on the shelf after he wiped it clean: aircraft systems, emergency medicine, coastal meteorology, one of Mara’s mathematics texts with a page marker made from an old fuel receipt.

Elias’s notebook remained wrapped in oilcloth.

Jane placed it on the shelf. Removed it. Set it inside the duffel. Removed it again. Finally he put it in the narrow space under the cot where water would not reach unless the hangar failed completely.

The barometer went on the wall last.

The brass rim looked wrong against corrugated metal, too fine and too damaged, from another life but not a softer one. He adjusted it until it hung level. The needle remained low. Falling, maybe. He would need an hour to confirm movement.

In the darkening hangar, the black Kitfox occupied most of the available world. It did not care what Ruth Calder suspected, what Wade had observed, what Anchorage might say, what the Coast Guard had done, or what the saint on the island flag appeared ready to defend by force. It cared about fuel, spark, air, compression, fabric tension, control cables, corrosion, weather, weight. Its moral universe was brutally clean. Neglect punished. Care accumulated. Error waited.

Jane put his palm on the left wing strut.

“You did fine,” he said quietly.

The words sounded ridiculous in the hangar. He had not meant to speak. He looked toward the open door, but the apron was empty. Beyond it, the rescue building glowed in yellow rectangles. The St. Jude flag outside had wrapped around its pole in the slackening wind, the saint hidden in folds.

He checked the brake line by headlamp. Fitting, as expected. Not loose enough to be called failure. Not tight enough to deserve trust. He wrote it on the back of an envelope, then added the rest of the morning’s work.

Left brake line — inspect fitting.

Ask Wade before using shop supplies.

Fuel water-check protocol.

Local frequencies.

Map: Apostle Cut / Black Friar / quarry road / ridge track.

Report possible visual before securing aircraft if active search.

Eat before flight.

He looked at the last two lines for a long time. Then he left them both.

Outside, the flag snapped loose from the pole and cracked once in the wind, sharp as a sheet of metal struck flat. Through the gap in the hangar door he could see the standard clearly for a moment in the field lights: dark blue, white border, St. Jude with his raised staff or weapon or whatever name the island had agreed to give the thing so it could remain devotional.

Jane watched until the cloth folded again and the figure vanished.

The Kitfox carried no such mark.

Not yet.

He closed the hangar door halfway, enough to cut the draft but not enough to trap fuel smell, then lay down on the cot in his clothes, bare feet cold beneath the blanket, the black wing above him holding the last of the hangar’s light. He did not belong to the island. The island did not belong to him. The plane did not need either proposition settled before morning.

For now, that was enough.
