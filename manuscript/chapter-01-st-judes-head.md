# Chapter One — St. Jude’s Head

Ten minutes after Jane arrived on St. Jude’s Head, the rain changed direction.

It had fallen straight through the landing, hard enough to bead on the black wings of the Kitfox and scatter across the windshield in silver threads while he taxied from the strip to the apron. Now it came sideways, finer and colder, driven off the ridge behind the airfield in sheets that could be watched as separate things moving over the ground. One crossed the flattened grass beyond the runway, darkened the fuel shed, blurred the municipal pickup parked beside the pump, and struck the aircraft with a soft needling force that made the fabric skin seem to absorb the weather rather than shine under it. The plane had been black before the rain. Under this sky it was almost depthless, a hard absence held together by struts, rivets, and the pale registration along the tail.

Jane stood beside the left wing and let his hand rest on the cowling seam until the last stored warmth from the engine disappeared. There was no useful reason to keep touching it. He knew the oil temperature had been good on shutdown, knew the left brake had gone slightly soft on taxi, knew the gust over the quarry had been worse than advisory and still not as bad as it might have been. He had logged the tach time in the small black notebook clipped inside the cockpit. He had set the control lock, covered the pitot tube, checked the door latch, and chocked the wheels. The machine had done what he had asked of it. Machines deserved to be left in a state that made the next request possible.

The island waited around him without welcome.

The airfield sat on a bench of cut ground above the harbor, as if someone had taken a knife to the hillside and decided civilization would fit if pressed hard enough. The runway was narrow, patched at both ends, and dark with rain. Three hangars faced the apron in a row, each repaired by a different decade and none of them convincingly winning its argument with weather. A low building with antennas bolted to its roof stood beyond the fuel pump. Its sign read ST. JUDE’S HEAD SEARCH AND RESCUE in blue letters that had lifted at the edges. Beside the door, on a short pole, the island flag snapped and folded in the wind: dark blue field, white border, the old figure of St. Jude in the center, robed and severe, one arm raised with a staff or weapon or some local distinction between the two. From a distance the halo looked less devotional than tactical.

The name had looked too elegant on charts. St. Jude’s Head. It belonged, at first glance, to a monastery, a private school, a chapel on a hill in some older country. It did not quite belong to the wet Alaskan promontory now showing itself in pieces through moving rain: black cliff, spruce, harbor cranes, rust marks below every hinge, an airstrip built where wind could get at it from three directions. That mismatch seemed to be part of the island’s pride. The flag appeared on the search-and-rescue building, on the municipal truck, on a faded sign by the fuel pump, and on the tails of two local aircraft tied down near the first hangar. The saint watched from each surface with the same hard patience, as if lost causes were not to be comforted but organized.

The Kitfox carried no such mark.

A gull landed on the hangar roof and began walking uphill against the weather. Its feet clicked over the corrugated metal with an absurd delicacy. Jane looked up at it, disliked the purposelessness of its movement, and bent to set the chocks more firmly.

“Most people tie down first.”

The voice came from behind him, close enough that he should have heard the approach. He stayed crouched until the chock was where he wanted it, then looked over his shoulder.

A woman stood at the edge of the apron in a dark orange waterproof jacket faded at the shoulders and elbows. Her hood hung down her back despite the rain. Her hair was short, mostly gray at the temples, and wet enough to lie flat against her head. She had the posture of someone who had spent a long time in places where expressive motion only gave the wind more to use. She was not looking at him in the usual way. Her attention had gone first to the plane, then to the knots, the angle of the tires, the open gravel near the threshold, his hands, his feet. Only after that did it settle on his face.

“I use both,” Jane said.

“I said first.”

“Tie-downs restrain the aircraft. Chocks keep it from making the tie-downs stupid.”

“Wind here makes everything stupid.”

“I read that.”

“You read that,” she said, with nothing in her voice to help him decide whether she meant to be amused.

Jane stood. Rain ran from his hair into the collar of his sweater. He had not put on shoes. His feet were pale against the wet gravel, reddening already from cold, but the ground was giving him useful information: bad drainage at the center of the apron, algae near the hangar threshold, a thin scatter of grit moved in short lines by gusts. Shoes dulled the world and then demanded gratitude for it.

The woman looked down once. Not long. A notation, not a question.

“Ruth Calder,” she said.

“Jane.”

“I know.”

Her hand was cold and firm. She released his quickly.

A man under the eave of the rescue building lifted a mug in what might have been greeting or warning. He was broad, bearded, wearing a fleece vest over a thermal shirt, his cap pulled low. Jane recognized the voice from the radio advisory during approach: calm through static, pretending the field had more official procedure than it did.

“That’s Lorne,” Ruth said. “He answers the radio when it suits him and authority when it doesn’t.”

“Airport advisory,” Lorne called. “Logistics. Maintenance. Moral witness.”

Jane looked at him. “Which one issued the wind report?”

“The one standing here in the rain.”

“That is the weakest form of authority.”

“It’s the only form that answered you.”

Ruth said, “Lorne.”

The mug lifted again, and Lorne retreated under the eave without moving his feet.

Jane returned to the aircraft. The left wing tie-down ring was slick beneath his fingers. He looped the strap, tightened, checked the angle against the present wind and the likely shift after dark. West later, perhaps northwest if the pressure kept falling. He had read old weather logs for this field until the island’s habits began to show through their omissions. The official station treated St. Jude’s Head as one place. It was not one place. It was a ridge, a harbor, a promontory, a quarry, a north cut, a west cliff, a low fog shelf, and several private arrangements between wind and rock. The numbers described what had happened near the instruments. They did not describe what the air might do to a light aircraft descending over the quarry.

Ruth watched him tie down. She did not interrupt.

“You came with paperwork,” she said when he finished.

“Yes.”

“And a plane.”

“Yes.”

“No car.”

“No.”

“No apartment.”

“I rented hangar two.”

“You rented storage.”

“I occupy space.”

“Not legally the same thing.”

“I’m aware.”

“Are you?”

He opened the baggage door and removed the folder wrapped in plastic, then a duffel, then a hard tool case. The medical kit remained inside. He did not like displaying medical equipment before he understood who protected which authority. Medicine made people either grateful or territorial, and both reactions damaged judgment if allowed too early.

Ruth noticed the case he did not remove. Her eyes touched it, went back to his face, and said nothing.

“Office,” she said.

“I need to put her inside.”

“She’ll survive rain.”

“She is not the issue.”

“What is?”

“Salt.”

“It’s an island.”

“I noticed.”

“You brought an airplane to an island and object to salt.”

“I object to pretending exposure is random.”

Lorne made a sound from under the eave.

Ruth did not smile. “Hangar door sticks.”

“Left track. Lift before pushing.”

Her expression changed slightly.

“I was here this morning,” Jane said. “By ferry.”

“For what?”

“To inspect the hangar.”

“You ferried in, rented the hangar, ferried back, then flew the aircraft over.”

“Yes.”

“With what sleep?”

“Some.”

“That’s not a number.”

The phrase struck harder than it should have. For a moment the rain lost texture, and another voice moved behind hers, colder, older, not similar except in the shape of correction. Elias had said that to him under clinic roofs, in supply rooms, beside fuel drums, over inventory sheets where fatigue had made arithmetic suspect. Not a number. Jane looked down at the duffel strap and made himself hold still until the old voice withdrew into the weather.

“Enough to land,” he said.

“That answer is designed to annoy me.”

“It wasn’t designed around you.”

“Then improve your design.”

Lorne crossed the apron to help with the hangar door, though no one had asked him and Jane had not invited him. Pride, weighed against wind and a wet threshold, produced poor results. Jane let him take the right strut when they rolled the Kitfox forward. The tires caught once at the lip where concrete met gravel; Lorne lifted at the wing strut, Jane pushed from the left, and the aircraft moved cleanly into hangar two.

The hangar swallowed the black plane in stages: propeller, cowling, windshield, wings, tail. Inside, the Kitfox seemed larger and stranger, the black skin gathering the dim light and holding it close. Rainwater dripped from the wing roots onto concrete. The place smelled of cold metal, old fuel, damp plywood, gulls somewhere above the roofline, and solvent that should have been sealed better. Against the north wall stood a warped workbench, two coils of rope no one should trust, a plastic crate of rusting hardware, and a calendar from nine years ago showing a charter boat in bright impossible weather. Behind a plywood partition was enough dry floor for a cot if the cot were placed diagonally.

“You staying here?” Lorne asked.

Jane checked the chocks again. “Yes.”

“No, he isn’t,” Ruth said from the doorway.

Jane kept working. “I am.”

“You rented storage.”

“I’m storing myself.”

Lorne took a slow drink from the mug he had brought with him. “I knew you were going to say that.”

Ruth stepped inside just far enough that rain struck the concrete behind her instead of her back. “This hangar has bad wiring, fuel residue, a west seam leak, and gulls in the roof.”

“Three gulls.”

Lorne looked up. “You counted?”

“They were loud.”

“The side door does not latch unless lifted,” Ruth said.

“I adjusted it this morning.”

“You adjusted municipal property.”

“It was wrong.”

“Municipal property can be wrong without becoming yours.”

“The door latches now.”

Ruth looked at Lorne.

Lorne looked toward the side door, then back at Jane with unwilling interest. “With what?”

“Shim. File.”

“You filed the door before meeting anyone?”

“I met the door.”

For the first time Ruth’s face almost changed enough to count as expression. It was gone quickly.

“Office,” she said again.

This time he followed.

The rain had thinned to a hard mist by the time they crossed to the rescue building. From the apron, the island fell away toward the harbor in wet terraces. Trucks moved below with headlights on. The ferry terminal was a pale rectangle near the water. Beyond it, fishing boats lay at the floats, their masts and rigging blurred by weather. Farther out, the sea broke white against a line of rock that appeared and disappeared as fog moved across it. Nothing seemed distant until the weather erased it. Then distance became absolute.

The search-and-rescue building opened into warmth and damp fabric. Jane stopped just inside because the room deserved to be read before anyone in it did.

Maps covered most of one wall. Not decorative maps. Working maps, layered with grease-pencil marks, clipped weather notes, tide windows, cliff access points, landing hazards, radio shadows, old wreck sites, private roads, unofficial names, and red circles around places whose meaning no one had written down because everyone local already knew it. A second wall held gear: helmets, flotation suits, harnesses, throw bags, medical packs, spare boots, radios in chargers, rain jackets with the St. Jude standard patched on the sleeve. Above the radio desk hung a framed version of the island flag. Indoors, the saint’s face looked narrower. The raised object in his hand looked less like a staff than anyone probably wanted to admit.

Two men at a side table were cleaning mud from rope. A woman with dark hair braided tight against her head entered figures into an old desktop computer. An older man slept in a chair beneath the coat hooks with his boots on and a handheld radio resting on his stomach. All of them looked at Jane. Not long. Long enough.

Ruth led him into a glass-walled office at the back. The blinds were bent. The desk was metal. The dehumidifier in the corner hummed with the exhausted optimism of an undersized machine. Binders lined one shelf, their labels curling from moisture. On the windowsill sat a cracked radio battery, a model floatplane, and a mug bearing the St. Jude figure in red. The mug saint looked even less merciful than the flag saint.

“Sit,” Ruth said.

Jane sat because standing would have made the chair important. It was uncomfortable in a deliberate way, its front edge pressing beneath the thigh, its back angled to discourage settlement. Ruth’s chair looked no better. That improved the office.

“Folder.”

He handed it over.

She removed the plastic wrap carefully. That mattered. People who treated documents as paper misunderstood them. Documents were instruments for moving permission, liability, and blame from one room to another. Mishandled, they failed as surely as a corroded switch.

Ruth began with the aircraft registration. Then insurance. Then maintenance. Her eyes slowed at the modification list: extended gear, larger tires, vortex generators, baggage alteration, fuel system notes, fabric repairs, modest avionics upgrade, transponder work, prop inspection. Lorne stood near the shelf pretending not to read over her shoulder. He was bad at pretending, but not aggressively bad.

“Black,” Ruth said.

Jane waited.

“You painted a working aircraft black.”

“Yes.”

“Over ocean.”

“Yes.”

“In weather.”

“The ocean is rarely above me.”

Lorne made a small sound into his mug.

Ruth looked up. “Search aircraft are sometimes searched for.”

“By then several decisions have already failed.”

“That is not an argument in favor.”

“No.”

“Why black?”

“It shows fluid leaks.”

“That your answer?”

“One of them.”

“What’s the other?”

Jane thought of the Kitfox in primer under shop lights, old color stripped away, fabric repairs sanded smooth under his fingers while Elias moved slowly around the workbench and pretended he was not moving slowly. He thought of choosing black because it ended conversation, because it made the plane severe, because it took away the cheerful small-aircraft prettiness other people expected and left the machine looking like what it had become to him: not transport, not hobby, not inheritance exactly, but a body whose survival could still be negotiated through work.

“It was available,” he said.

Ruth did not believe him. That was fine. He had not believed himself for years.

She reached the pilot documents.

The office became very specific.

Jane watched the peeled corner of laminate on the desk rather than her face. The papers were good. Good enough for people already inclined to be done with paperwork. Not good enough to survive every phone call. Some details were true, some true only in the way a shoreline was true at one tide, some assembled from systems that did not communicate well across distance and death. The trouble with documents was not making them plausible. The trouble was that every plausible thing required maintenance.

Ruth read the license copy once. Then again.

“Originals?” she asked.

“Stored.”

“That word doing work.”

“Yes.”

“Where?”

“Not here.”

“In Alaska?”

He could have said Anchorage. He could have said with a lawyer. He could have said bank box, archive, family friend, any of several answers with clean edges and future consequences. He had slept too little to begin maintaining fresh lies unless they improved something important.

“No,” he said.

“At least honest.”

“Not necessarily.”

Lorne laughed under his breath. Ruth ignored him.

“You understand we coordinate with state troopers, Coast Guard, EMS, and occasionally FAA when someone bends enough metal to interest them.”

“Yes.”

“And you came here anyway.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“You need pilots.”

“I need a roof that doesn’t leak into the server closet. Need is not an invitation.”

“It often becomes one.”

“Not in writing.”

“No.”

Ruth went deeper into the folder. Medical letters now. Field clinics overseas. Organizations with inconsistent formatting and signatures from people whose titles meant different things depending on the country, the week, and the generator. Elias’s letter was there too, scanned from clinic stationery, his handwriting narrow and abrupt in the margin where he had corrected a date. Jane had not read it since the funeral. It described skill without warmth and responsibility without praise. Elias alive in the most Elias way possible: precise, withholding, useful after leaving.

“Elias Venn,” Ruth said.

“Yes.”

“Doctor.”

“Yes.”

“Guardian.”

“Yes.”

Her eyes moved to the final page. “Dead.”

The word did not become gentler because it was accurate.

“Yes.”

“When?”

“Six weeks.”

Lorne shifted near the shelves. Outside the glass, someone in the main room set down a cup too hard, then quieted it with a hand. Jane felt the room beyond the office rearrange around the new information. Not gossip first. Awareness first. Fact enters, person re-sorted, eyes redirected too late.

“Sorry,” Ruth said.

Jane nodded because it cost less than speech.

She read Elias’s letter to the end and placed it beside the license copy rather than on top of it. The separation was deliberate.

“You are not medical staff here.”

“No.”

“You do not present yourself as medical staff.”

“No.”

“If someone is hurt, you tell my medic what you know and do what the lead tells you.”

“What if the lead is wrong?”

Lorne sighed at the ceiling.

Ruth’s face remained still. “Then you will have encountered one of civilization’s older design flaws. You will still not make it worse for sport.”

“I don’t do things for sport.”

“You flew here barefoot in a black aircraft under lowering weather with paperwork that asks me to do arithmetic in public.”

“That wasn’t sport.”

“No,” Ruth said. “That was worse.”

Through the office window, Jane could see hangar two and the Kitfox’s black tail in shadow. Beyond it, the two local aircraft wore the St. Jude emblem near their doors, one decal peeling, another repainted with care. His aircraft looked bare beside them, not unfinished exactly, but unwilling.

Ruth followed his glance. “You don’t have the standard.”

“No.”

“Most local pilots do.”

“I’m not local.”

“Obviously.”

Lorne said, “Some put him on before they learn to land here. Usually those are the ones who ground-loop in front of the fuel pump.”

Jane looked at him. “Does he help with taxi?”

“Not historically.”

“Then I’ll wait.”

Ruth closed the folder. “Here is what I can offer without lying to anyone in a way that becomes a meeting. Ground probation. Radio log under supervision. Gear inventory. Map familiarization. Field maintenance if Lorne permits it. Observation only on calls unless a lead authorizes more. You do not fly for this organization. You do not transport patients under this organization. You do not self-dispatch because something on the radio makes you feel useful.”

“I don’t feel useful.”

“Then because something on the radio makes you feel irritated.”

He said nothing. That was closer.

“One week,” Ruth said. “Then reassessment.”

Lorne looked at her. “One?”

“He’ll become a liability faster if bored.”

“I’m good at boredom,” Jane said.

“No,” Ruth and Lorne said together.

That annoyed him because it had rhythm.

“Shoes in the main building during calls,” Ruth said. “Gear room, clinic, fuel area, broken glass, metal work, cliff calls, boat calls if marine lead says so. Hangar is your stupidity.”

“Public events?” Lorne asked.

“No,” Jane said.

Ruth looked at him. “We have not discussed public events.”

“I’m pre-refusing.”

“Efficient,” Lorne said.

“Fine,” Ruth said, surprising both of them. “If you get tetanus, I am not filling out the report.”

“That’s not how reports work.”

“Then don’t get tetanus.”

The radio in the main room changed tone.

Not volume. Tone. A channel opening under wind, then a voice cut by static. Everyone outside the office adjusted. The two men at the rope table stopped moving. The woman at the computer put one hand to her headset. The older man beneath the coat hooks opened his eyes as if waking had been part of the equipment checklist.

Ruth left the office immediately. Lorne followed. Jane remained seated for half a breath, then stood and went after them.

The woman at the radio was already writing. “Repeat location.”

Static broke over the room. A man’s voice returned, older, strained more by weather than panic. “North side. Apostle Cut, maybe half mile east. Morrison boys overdue from camp. Engine trouble before signal dropped. Two aboard. No beacon. Handheld maybe dead.”

Ruth was at the wall map. “Time of last contact?”

The question went out. The answer came back in pieces. Forty minutes. Small skiff. Two brothers, local, coming back with supplies. West wind building. Tide rising. Apostle Cut lay along the northern side of the island where the coast folded inward through rock teeth and kelp beds. Jane had studied it because every forum, ferry complaint, accident report, and harbor notice mentioned it with the same mixture of boredom and fear. Water did not have to be dramatic there to become complicated.

No one asked him anything.

Ruth began issuing instructions. Marine team assembling. North overlook truck. Clinic standby. Coast Guard monitoring. Weather below helicopter minimums for the moment. The last part carried no contempt. Minimums were not cowardice. They were one system of survival failing to align with another.

Jane looked through the window toward hangar two. The black nose of the Kitfox was just visible in shadow. From the airfield to Apostle Cut, low observation, direct, twelve minutes if the ceiling held. Less if he accepted turbulence he should not accept. No landing. Mark position. Relay coordinates. Search the kelp line, Black Friar Rock, lee side of the outer teeth. He could be airborne before the harbor boat cleared the breakwater.

His body had already begun the sequence.

“Stop,” Lorne said quietly.

Jane turned.

Lorne stood close enough that the word did not carry. His orange jacket was half zipped, his radio clipped high on his chest. The St. Jude patch on his cap had darkened with rain, the little armed figure reduced to white thread and judgment.

“I didn’t move,” Jane said.

“You moved in your head.”

“That does not affect anyone.”

“It will if your feet follow.”

Jane looked back at the map. “Your boat team will be slow.”

“Yes.”

“I can be there faster.”

“Yes.”

“Then—”

“No.”

It was not Ruth who said it. Ruth was on the radio, back turned. Lorne said it quietly, almost gently, which made it more difficult to dismiss.

“People could die while you preserve procedure,” Jane said.

“People could die if every useful stranger becomes an aircraft.”

“I’m not a stranger to my aircraft.”

“You’re a stranger to us.”

True. Inconveniently placed.

Ruth finished transmitting and turned. “Jane.”

He looked at her.

“Radio desk. Assist Ana with logging. Do not transmit unless told.”

The woman at the radio, Ana, slid a blank log sheet toward the second chair without looking up. “Time, speaker, content. If you don’t hear, write unclear. Don’t invent.”

“I know.”

“Good for you.”

He sat. The spare headset pressed one ear sore almost immediately. He wrote the date, time, channel, initial report. His handwriting became very small, the spacing even. Weather, location, last contact, persons aboard, no beacon, uncertain handheld battery. At 15:42, harbor team departing. At 15:45, last known point repeated. At 15:47, Coast Guard monitoring, no launch. At 15:51, north overlook truck en route. At 15:54, under static, a fragment came through: “…black rock…can’t hold…”

Jane wrote: unclear / possible black rock / verify.

Ana leaned over, read it, and raised the mic. “Station to harbor, did anyone copy reference to black rock?”

Negative from harbor. Then, half a minute later, another station broke in. Morrison camp confirmed the brothers sometimes cut inside Black Friar Rock in west wind.

Ruth looked up from the map.

Jane did not.

He wrote: Black Friar Rock. Local route. West wind. Camp confirms.

No one praised him. Good.

The call lengthened. The boat team slowed outside the breakwater. Fog lowered on the north ridge. The Coast Guard requested updates it could not yet act on. Ruth sent the overlook truck higher and requested a portable repeater. Jane kept writing. At 16:14, the boat team asked whether air observation was available if the ceiling permitted.

The room held still.

Ruth did not look at Jane. That was worse than looking.

“Negative air,” she said into the radio. “No cleared asset available.”

The sentence passed through him cleanly and left too many meanings behind. Accurate. False. Legal. Ornamental. Necessary. Cowardly. Sensible. All at once, which made it impossible to hate with any efficiency.

Ana wrote the sentence exactly.

Jane looked down at his hands. They had closed without permission. He opened them and returned to the log.

At 16:22, north overlook reported poor visibility.

At 16:25, boat team diverted toward Black Friar Rock.

At 16:31, one red flare reported north of the rock.

The room exhaled without relaxing.

Once a point existed, the system reorganized around it. Coordinates. Boat heading. Clinic standby confirmed. Ambulance to harbor. Ruth’s voice remained flat, abbreviated, conserving everything except command. Jane wrote until his wrist ached. He did not look at the Kitfox again. Looking would not change the rule. It would only make him worse at the task in front of him.

At 16:49, the boat team located the skiff. Disabled, taking water, both aboard, one injured, both cold.

At 17:08, transfer complete.

At 17:26, boat team inbound slow.

At 17:41, ambulance dispatched to harbor.

Only when Ruth said, “Maintain log, stand down air consideration,” did Jane realize his shoulders hurt from holding still.

Ana removed her headset and flexed her fingers. “You write like a tax auditor.”

“Is that bad?”

“Legible. So, spiritually, yes.”

Ruth came to the desk after the ambulance had left for the clinic. Wet had darkened her jacket nearly brown at the shoulders though she had never gone beyond the building. She read the log page by page, one hand braced on the table. At the Black Friar line, she paused.

“You caught that?”

“Yes.”

“Ana verified?”

“Yes,” Ana said. “He heard it under garbage static. Annoying.”

Ruth kept reading. “Good.”

Jane nodded once before he could stop himself.

Ruth looked at him. “You wanted to fly.”

“Yes.”

“You didn’t.”

“No.”

“Why?”

“You gave an instruction.”

“That has not historically stopped people.”

“It was recent.”

Ana made a sound that might have been a laugh if she had respected it more.

“Try for permanent,” Ruth said.

The first truck returned from the north overlook with its headlights smeared by rain. Lorne climbed out stiffly, one hand pressing his cap down against the wind. He looked toward hangar two first, then through the window at Jane still seated at the radio desk. Seeing him there, he nodded once. Not approval. Confirmation.

Jane looked away before it could become something.

The rescued brothers arrived by harbor ambulance twenty minutes later, wet, shivering, angry in the weak automatic way of men embarrassed to have needed saving. One had gashed his forearm on a cleat when the skiff took a wave sideways. Not arterial. Dirty. Poor pressure wrap. Jane stood before he decided to, saw Ruth notice him, and sat back down.

Ana noticed too. She said nothing.

After the brothers were moved to the clinic, the building degraded slowly from emergency into aftermath. Radios returned to chargers. Jackets hung near the heater. Someone made coffee no one praised. The tide board was updated. The grease-pencil marks came off the map. The completed log went into a tray by Ruth’s office door.

Jane remained at the desk until there was no task left. That was the dangerous moment. Without task, his body remembered the crossing, the ferry, the bad sleep, the missed food, the cold concrete waiting in hangar two, the papers in Ruth’s folder, the fact of Elias folded somewhere behind all of it like a chart he refused to open.

He stood too quickly. The room shifted once at the edges. He corrected with one hand on the desk, hated the correction, and removed the hand.

Ruth saw. Of course.

“Eat,” she said.

“I have food.”

“Where?”

“Plane.”

“That means no.”

“I said I have food.”

“And I translated.”

She pointed toward a slow cooker on the side counter beside stacked paper bowls. The smell reached him only after she named it: fish, potatoes, onion, pepper, smoke. Hunger, having been ignored long enough to become background, returned as irritation.

“I’ll eat later.”

“You’ll eat now or I’ll revise probation to include being fed by Lorne.”

Lorne, across the room, said, “I’m not gentle.”

Jane took a bowl.

He ate standing near the map because sitting seemed like surrender. The stew was too hot at first, then good enough to be offensive. Halibut broke apart against the spoon. Pepper cleared the cold from his throat. Someone had made food in quantity because people here expected weather and hunger to coincide. Intelligent.

As he ate, he studied the map without appearing to. St. Jude’s Head on paper looked narrower than from the air, its western promontory clawing into the Gulf, its interior cut by bog, ridge, quarry road, old timber tracks, and names that carried too much church or not enough: Apostle Cut, Black Friar Rock, Mercy Cove, Sisters Ridge, Anselm Quarry, Old Cannery Road. The island seemed to have baptized every hazard and then continued losing people in them.

Above the radio desk, the framed standard shifted slightly in a draft. St. Jude’s face caught the overhead light, stern and elongated. Jane wondered who had decided the figure needed to look armed. He wondered how many committee meetings had preserved that decision under the word tradition. He wondered why every local aircraft except his carried the mark somewhere near the door or tail. He wondered, briefly and with irritation, whether the absence on the Kitfox had already been read.

Let them read it.

The plane was not theirs.

Ruth appeared beside him without ceremony. “Seven tomorrow.”

“For what?”

“Map work. Gear inventory. Radio protocol. Lorne wants to argue about your brake line.”

“It’s not an argument if I’m right.”

“That sentence will shorten your life.”

“I doubt it.”

“You doubt usefully or recreationally?”

“Both.”

She looked at the map with him for a moment. Outside, the rain thinned enough to show the runway as a black line under low sky. The Kitfox sat visible through the hangar opening, nose inward, tail toward gray light. It looked less like it had arrived than like it had been uncovered.

“St. Jude’s Head makes people declare themselves eventually,” Ruth said.

Jane did not answer.

She nodded once toward the wall flag. “Not with that. With weather.”

“I don’t declare easily.”

“No,” she said. “I noticed.”

When he returned to hangar two, evening had lowered over the airfield though the clock objected. Water dripped from roof edges, struts, cables, gutters, the whole field ticking and clicking in the cold. Harbor lights burned below as yellow stains behind fog. Somewhere down there, the rescued brothers were being warmed, bandaged, scolded, and claimed. Somewhere beyond the breakwater, the water continued trying to take back the route they had used.

Jane stood inside the hangar door and listened to the building. Three gulls in the roof. Wind at the west seam. Water collecting behind the partition but not crossing it. Door latch holding, imperfectly. Aircraft cooling. His own breathing too shallow from fatigue.

He set the empty bowl on the workbench, disliked it there, carried it back to the rescue building’s side door, washed it in the utility sink, dried it, and returned before anyone could comment.

Then he unpacked.

Cot behind the partition where the floor stayed dry. Sleeping bag. One folded blanket. Tool case beneath the bench. Spare parts in a plastic bin. Books on the shelf after he wiped it clean: aircraft systems, emergency medicine, coastal meteorology, one of Mara’s mathematics texts with a page marker made from an old fuel receipt. Elias’s notebook remained wrapped in oilcloth. Jane placed it on the shelf, removed it, placed it inside the duffel, removed it again, and finally set it in the narrow space under the cot where water would not reach unless the entire hangar failed.

The barometer went on the wall last.

The brass rim looked wrong against corrugated metal, too fine and too damaged, from another life but not a softer one. He adjusted it until it hung level. The needle remained low. Falling, maybe. He would need an hour to confirm movement.

In the darkening hangar, the black Kitfox occupied most of the available world. It did not care what Ruth Calder suspected, what Lorne had observed, what the state required, what the Coast Guard declined, or what the saint on the island flag appeared ready to defend by force. It cared about fuel, spark, air, compression, fabric tension, control cables, corrosion, weather, weight. Its moral universe was brutally clean. Neglect punished. Care accumulated. Error waited.

Jane put his palm on the left wing strut.

“You did fine,” he said quietly.

The words sounded ridiculous in the hangar. He had not meant to speak. He looked toward the open door, but the apron was empty. Beyond it, the rescue building glowed in yellow rectangles. The St. Jude flag outside had wrapped around its pole in the slackening wind, the saint hidden in folds.

He closed the hangar door halfway, enough to cut the draft but not enough to trap fuel smell. Then he checked the brake line by headlamp, wiped moisture from the cowling seam, and made a list for morning on the back of an old envelope.

Left brake line — inspect fitting.

Door track — ask before filing further.

Fuel water-check protocol.

Local frequencies.

Map: Apostle Cut / Black Friar / north overlook.

Do not transmit unless told.

Eat before flight.

He looked at the last line, annoyed, then left it.

Outside, the flag snapped loose from the pole and cracked once in the wind, sharp as a sheet of metal struck flat. Through the gap in the hangar door he could see the standard clearly for a moment in the field lights: dark blue, white border, St. Jude with his raised weapon or staff or whatever name the island had agreed to give the thing so it could remain devotional.

Jane watched until the cloth folded again and the figure vanished.

Then he lay down on the cot in his clothes, bare feet cold beneath the blanket, the black wing above him holding the last of the hangar’s light. He did not belong to the island. The island did not belong to him. The plane did not need either proposition settled before morning.

For now, that was enough.
