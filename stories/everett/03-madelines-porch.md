# Everett — Madeline’s Porch

Madeline’s porch light clicked every nine seconds.

Everett counted it before he knew he was counting. The sound came from the fixture above the front door, a cheap brass-colored thing with frosted glass and two dead moths lying in the bowl of it like offerings that had failed to impress anyone. Click. Pause. Wind through the cottonwood. A truck passing two blocks over. Click. The porch boards had been painted blue at some point and then surrendered unevenly to weather, so that each board held its own history of exposure: flakes curled up along nail heads, bare wood silvered at the edges, old paint darker near the wall where rain reached less often. A porch swing hung from chains on the left, one link replaced with a carabiner. The mailbox leaned. The whole house gave the impression of having been repaired by people who cared and could not afford to care comprehensively.

Madeline stood with her keys in her hand and did not ask him if he liked it. That was one of the first things Everett noticed. Adults asked children whether they liked places when the answer had no power. Do you like your room, do you like this school, do you like your new church, do you like these shoes, do you like the life that has already been arranged around you. Madeline only looked at the porch light as it clicked and said, “I swear I fixed that.”

Tina, behind him, made a tired sound that might have been a laugh if she had not used the last twelve hours filing pieces of a catastrophe into temporary categories. “You said that last time.”

“Last time it was doing a different bad thing.” Madeline glanced at Everett, not for approval, more as if checking whether the volume of her voice had startled him. “This one is new. Technically progress.”

Everett said nothing. He held the paper grocery bag she had given him at the county building because refusing it had seemed more conspicuous than accepting it. Inside were oranges, a loaf of bread, cinnamon rolls wrapped in foil, and a jar of peanut butter with the red seal unbroken. The bag had warmed against his side. Grease from the rolls had darkened one corner. The smell of cinnamon followed him up the steps with an intimacy he disliked.

The neighborhood was not rural enough to be safe by New Mercy standards and not urban enough to hide easily. Narrow lots. Chain-link fences. Detached garages. Cars parked nose-out as if everyone might have to leave quickly but had agreed not to mention it. Across the street, a woman pulled a curtain aside and let it fall. Two houses down, the communist dog began barking at the mailbox or at Everett or at the idea of federal mail generally. Its bark was frantic and offended. Madeline lifted one hand in that direction.

“Walter, your politics are incoherent,” she called.

The dog barked harder.

Tina looked at Everett. “You’ll be here tonight. I’ll come by in the morning. There’s a number on the fridge. Mine, the after-hours line, and the placement office. If anything feels unsafe, you call.”

Everett looked at the windows. Living room front. Kitchen side. Likely bedroom above porch. Basement windows half-height, one painted shut, one cracked. Back fence old cedar, six feet, gate latch visible from driveway. Alley access. Detached garage with padlock but no car inside from the sound of it. The house was not defensible, but most houses weren’t. They were built for weather and privacy and the sustained delusion that doors decided things.

“Unsafe how?” he asked.

Tina paused. He had meant the question plainly. She heard more inside it. Adults did that when they were trying. “Any way that matters,” she said.

That was a bad answer and a good one. He filed it under both.

Madeline unlocked the door and stepped in first. Good again. People who gestured for him to enter before them wanted to watch his back. People who entered first exposed theirs. Either she knew that or she was careless in a useful direction. The house smelled of coffee, dust, lemon dish soap, and the old electric heat that collected in baseboard registers even after the season had warmed. Lamps were already on inside. Not overhead lights. Lamps. Their shades made yellow pools against the walls and left corners intact. Everett stood just inside the threshold while Tina came in behind him and shut the door softly.

The living room had a couch with a sag in the middle, two mismatched chairs, milk crates full of records, a low bookcase, a television with a crack in the lower corner of the screen, and a blanket folded over the armrest with the precision of someone who had learned tidiness as apology rather than doctrine. On the wall above the couch hung three framed prints: a black horse in snow, a river under a bridge, and a photograph of a woman in a marching band uniform holding a trumpet and squinting into sun. No scripture boards. No chore charts. No posted rules except a faded magnet on the refrigerator visible through the kitchen doorway: DON’T BE A DICK, in cheerful block letters.

Everett looked at it too long.

Madeline followed his gaze. “House constitution,” she said. “Imperfectly enforced.”

Tina set her purse on the kitchen counter. “Madeline.”

“What? It’s aspirational.”

The exchange had the softness of people who had disagreed before and survived it. Everett placed the grocery bag on the table because his hand had begun to ache. The table was round and scarred by heat rings. One chair had a green cushion tied badly to the back. On the counter, a pot sat with tomato sauce dried along its rim. In the sink were three dishes soaking, not hidden, not displayed as confession. The ordinary disorder of the place unsettled him more than filth would have. Filth meant neglect. Order meant control. This was neither. This was a person living without turning every object into evidence.

Tina opened a folder and reviewed practicalities with Madeline in the kitchen: medication, no visitors, no media, no unsupervised transport beyond what had been agreed, emergency hearing Monday, sheriff contact, attorney contact once assigned. Everett listened from the edge of the living room and watched the front window. The curtain moved when the baseboard heat clicked on. Outside, evening had lowered itself into the street. A sedan slowed near the curb, continued. A boy on a bicycle coasted past standing on one pedal. The porch light clicked.

Madeline said, “He can lock the bedroom door?”

Tina lowered her voice. “Yes.”

“I mean from the inside.”

“Yes.”

“Okay.” A pause. “And I can take the knob off my side?”

Everett turned his head before Tina answered.

Madeline looked at him directly, then shrugged as if embarrassed by her own thought. “It has one of those little emergency holes outside. I can tape over it or replace it tomorrow. Or not. I don’t know what’s better. I just don’t want you thinking I can pop it open whenever.”

Everett could not locate the trick. “Why would you do that?”

“Because it’s creepy?” she said.

“No. Why would you care what I think you can open?”

Madeline’s face changed by fractions. The easy humor remained available but stepped back, giving the room to something sturdier. “Because it’s your door.”

No one spoke for a moment. Tina looked down at the papers. Everett looked at Madeline. The statement was false in every legal, financial, and practical sense. The door belonged to Madeline, or to a bank, or to a landlord, or to whatever adult name existed on county forms. It belonged to the house. It belonged to the state’s temporary permission. It did not belong to him. But she had said it in the small domestic sense, the way a cup might be his because she put it in front of him and did not take it back without asking. He distrusted that sense most of all because it could become real before anyone had earned it.

Tina left at 7:43.

Everett watched from the living room window as her car pulled away. She did not sit idling in front of the house. She did not look back dramatically. She checked the mirror, signaled, and entered traffic with bureaucratic dignity. Her taillights diminished past Walter’s fence. The dog barked at them until they were gone and then seemed confused by the loss of purpose.

Madeline stayed in the kitchen with both hands braced on the counter. She did not ask if he was okay. He had begun to suspect she collected better questions by refusing the obvious ones.

“I’m making grilled cheese,” she said. “You can eat it or not eat it. No speech attached.”

“I already ate.”

“A county sandwich.”

He said nothing.

“That’s not food. That’s paperwork with mayonnaise.”

The sentence reached him before he could defend against it. A laugh almost occurred. It stopped somewhere below his throat and became only a brief change in breathing. Madeline heard it and did not celebrate. Good again.

She took bread from the bag, then cheese from the refrigerator. She moved around the kitchen with a competence that was not graceful but complete. Pan from lower cabinet. Butter dish. Plate. Knife. She hummed badly under her breath and stopped when she noticed herself doing it. Everett stood near the living room doorway and catalogued implements by habit: knife, glass jar, ceramic mug, cast-iron pan too heavy for fast use but effective if required, kettle, scissors in drawer half-open, back door deadbolt cheap, window over sink painted shut. He knew how to move through the room if it changed. He knew where to stand so the hallway remained accessible. None of this required panic. It was only maintenance.

“Do you want water?” Madeline asked.

“Yes.”

“Tap okay?”

The question came so casually that for an instant he thought she had been briefed on the well, the cistern, the hidden tank, the county inspector, the spring beyond the ridge. Then she turned with a glass in her hand and looked merely uncertain.

“Yes,” he said.

She filled the glass and set it on the table, then stepped away from it. He waited until her back turned before drinking. The water tasted faintly of chlorine and metal. City water. Public water. Treated, pumped, billed, governed. He drank half and set the glass down exactly where she had placed it.

The sandwich browned in the pan. Butter hissed. The smell filled the kitchen, immediate and childish, and he resented it for knowing a version of him that had never existed. Madeline slid the sandwich onto a plate, cut it in half, then seemed to reconsider and cut one half again into two triangles. She put the plate at the seat nearest him and took the chair across the table with her own sandwich. She did not pray. She did not wait. She bit into hers, burned her mouth, and whispered a word Sister Agnes would have assigned a fast for.

Everett looked at her.

Madeline swallowed carefully. “That was between me and the cheese.”

He sat down because standing had become too much like an announcement. The chair creaked under him. He picked up one of the smaller triangles and examined the melted edge where cheese had escaped and crisped against the pan. No one watched his mouth. Madeline looked at her own plate with deliberate interest. He ate. The first bite was too hot and almost too rich. His body, traitorous and practical, wanted the rest.

They ate in silence for a while. Outside, a car door slammed. The porch light clicked. The refrigerator motor came on with a shudder and settled into an uneven hum. Everett let the sounds arrange themselves. Houses spoke constantly if no one trained them not to.

Madeline said, “Tina told me some things.”

He put the sandwich down.

“Not everything,” she said. “Probably not even close. Enough that I’m not going to ask stupid questions tonight.”

“What are stupid questions?”

“Questions people ask because they want to feel brave for hearing the answer.”

He considered her more carefully. The dyed hair, the boots, the rings, the tattoo at her wrist, the softness that ruined all of it as camouflage. She was not frightening. She was worse: difficult to classify.

“I don’t need you to tell me anything,” she said. “I do need to say house stuff. Bathroom is down the hall. Your room is upstairs on the right. My room is at the other end. I sleep with a fan on, so if you need me, knock loud or yell. If you don’t want to yell, throw something at the wall. Not, like, the lamp. Something affordable.”

He stared.

“Sorry. Jokes when nervous. Working on it.”

“You’re afraid of me.”

Madeline did not deny it quickly. Another point in her favor. She looked at the bitten edge of her sandwich, then at him. “A little. Not in the way people probably think.”

“What way?”

“I’m afraid I’ll do the wrong thing because I think I’m helping.”

That answer had no obvious use. He distrusted it less than he expected to.

“You should be afraid of me in the way people think,” he said.

“Okay.”

“Okay?”

“I heard you.” She leaned back, not far, just enough for the chair to complain. “I’m not going to argue you into being harmless. That sounds exhausting for both of us.”

The room went quiet around the shape of the sentence. Everett felt the old machinery in him search for an opening. Adults corrected his self-description when it made them uncomfortable. They insisted he was confused, wounded, manipulated, a good boy underneath, a monster, a victim, an asset, a liar, a miracle, damned, salvageable, depending on which institution had provided their vocabulary. Madeline did not choose one. She let the statement stand without letting it own the table.

He picked up the sandwich again. “I’m not harmless.”

“I believe you.”

“Then why am I here?”

“Because not harmless and not worth feeding are different categories.”

There it was again, the almost-laugh, nearer this time and more painful for being nearer. He took another bite to stop it.

After dinner, she showed him the room. The stairs rose steeply from the hall, carpet worn in the center. At the top were three doors and a linen closet with a broken handle. His room had a bed, a dresser, a lamp, and a window looking over the driveway and detached garage. The bedspread was blue plaid. On the dresser lay two clean towels, a toothbrush still in packaging, a bar of soap, and a cheap spiral notebook with a pen placed on top. Madeline stood in the hallway, not crossing the threshold.

“Notebook’s yours,” she said. “For writing or tearing up or putting under the wobbly dresser leg. No wrong answer.”

He walked to the window. The backyard was small and darkening. Along the fence, weeds grew through gravel. A bicycle frame leaned against the garage. The gate to the alley hung slightly open. He noted the distance from the window to the porch roof below. Manageable, if necessary. Painful with the wrist, but manageable.

“The window opens,” Madeline said from the hall. “Screen sticks. If you smoke, don’t set the curtains on fire. Also you’re fourteen, so legally pretend I didn’t say that.”

“I don’t smoke.”

“Good. Then definitely don’t set the curtains on fire.”

He turned. “Why are you like this?”

The question came out sharper than he intended, but Madeline only looked at him for a while. Her face, unarranged by humor now, seemed younger and older at once. “Because when everything is serious, I start making it worse,” she said. “So I leave a little room around it.”

He did not understand the method, but he understood methods.

She tapped the doorframe once with her knuckle. “I’ll be downstairs. You can close this.”

“Lock it?”

“Yeah.”

He waited.

Madeline took the hint after a second and backed down the hall. “Right. Proving the concept.” She descended the stairs, each step announcing the limits of the house. In the kitchen below, a faucet ran. A cabinet opened. The porch light clicked through the window glass, softer here but still exact.

Everett shut the door. He turned the lock. Then he stood with his hand on the knob and waited for the punishment inside the gesture. None came. No Sister opened it. No Mother called from below. No voice corrected the arrogance of a locked door. The room held. The lock held. The house continued to make its small undisciplined sounds.

He crossed to the dresser and picked up the notebook. The first page was blank. Not lined with doctrine. Not assigned. Blank in a way that seemed almost hostile. He set it down again.

At his wrist, beneath the edge of bandage and borrowed sleeve, the first visible color of the tattooing showed: a green so bright it did not belong to county offices, foster houses, or burned settlements. The marks had been made to claim him, to render him readable to people who knew the pattern. In the yellow lamplight of Madeline’s spare room they looked merely beautiful, which felt like another kind of lie. He pushed the sleeve down.

Outside, Walter barked once in his sleep or in principle. The refrigerator hummed below. Madeline moved around the kitchen, then the living room, then stopped. After a while he heard the faint scratch of a record beginning badly, the needle catching a groove, music rising soft and warped through the floorboards. Trumpet, maybe. Or a voice made thin by old speakers. She had not turned it up enough to enter the room without permission.

Everett sat on the bed without taking off his shoes. The mattress dipped under him. He watched the locked door until the porch light clicked one hundred times. At ninety-seven, he realized he had stopped planning how to leave. At one hundred, he began again, because stopping was dangerous. But the plans came slower now, having to move through grilled cheese, city water, the ridiculous dog, the constitution magnet, Madeline’s voice from the hallway saying it’s your door as if the world were capable of making such a thing true for one night.

He did not sleep for a long time.

When sleep came at last, it did not come as surrender. It came as a mechanical failure, the body taking authority while the mind looked away. He lay on top of the blue plaid bedspread with his shoes still on and the lamp still burning, one hand tucked beneath the pillow, facing the door. Downstairs, the record ended and clicked softly in its own circle until Madeline lifted the needle. The porch light outside continued its defective counting, patient and cheap and faithful to nothing but electricity.
