# Everett — The Dry Well

The dry well was not dry in any visible way. It still held a skin of black water ten feet below the stones, and on windless mornings it gave back a narrow circle of sky so clean that the younger children sometimes leaned over the curb to look at it until one of the Sisters took them by the shoulders and moved them away. But the pump had begun to catch. That was the phrase Mother Ester used, as if the trouble were moral and could be corrected by patience. The pump catches, she said, and the girls who drew water for the kitchen learned to pull the handle in shorter strokes, with their faces blank and their calves braced against the gravel, listening for the hollow cough below. Water came anyway, because water was required, because the settlement had no appetite for the meanings attached to failure. Everett watched them from the shade side of the washhouse with a galvanized bucket between his ankles and a bruise at his left wrist where Sister Agnes had corrected his grip that morning. The bruise was small, not worth thinking about. It had a pleasing color, almost violet at the center, fading toward yellow. He liked exact colors because colors did not lie unless someone named them wrong.

Beyond the washhouse, the steppe ran out flat and dun under a sky that had too much room in it. The buildings of New Mercy stood as if they had been set down by a careful hand and then dared to remain: chapel, schoolhouse, infirmary, tool barn, dormitories, Mothers’ residence, smokehouse, dry storage, the old radio shed with its antenna wired to a mast that sang whenever the wind rose from the south. Every structure had been repaired in mismatched eras. New corrugated panels flashed beside sun-split pine. Whitewash peeled under fresh black stencils. A water board notice, county-printed and laminated, had been pinned crookedly to the kitchen door for three weeks, though no one read it after the first day. The Sisters left it there because the county liked to see its own language honored. Everett had learned that obedience could be theatrical. A sign did not have to be believed in order to be useful.

Mother Serene came across the yard in her gray dress and work boots, moving with the frictionless economy that made even gravel seem quieter under her. Beside her walked the county man. He was not old, but he had the prematurely careful posture of someone who spent much of his life sitting in trucks and municipal rooms where men argued about budgets with their arms folded over coffee-stained shirts. His face was pink above his collar. He carried a clipboard in one hand and a plastic water bottle in the other, which he kept forgetting not to drink from in front of people who had been told the cistern would be sufficient through August. Everett noticed the bottle first. It was not sin that made it interesting. It was inventory.

“Chauncey,” Mother Serene called without raising her voice.

A man emerged from the tool barn, wiping both hands on a rag. Chauncey had been with them since before Everett could remember him, which meant Everett did not ask where he had come from. Adults who had no past were common at New Mercy. They accumulated there with new names and old useful skills. Chauncey knew engines, culverts, locks, septic pumps, and the private temperaments of every road within fifteen miles. He could make a machine run while insulting it in a whisper. The Sisters did not rebuke him for profanity when no children were near, which taught Everett that rules had load ratings like bridges.

“The inspector wants to see the east cistern,” Mother Serene said.

“Of course.” Chauncey looked once at the county man, then at Mother Serene, then at Everett. It was not a long look. Nothing in New Mercy was allowed to become long until a Mother made it so.

Everett picked up the bucket.

“Not that,” Mother Serene said. “Come here.”

He set the bucket down without hurry and crossed the yard. At thirteen, he had already learned to keep his shoulders relaxed under observation. Children who stiffened made adults want to explain things to them, and explanations were often traps. He stopped two paces behind Mother Serene and folded his hands at the base of his spine as he had been taught. The county man smiled at him. The expression was automatic and weak, like a porch light left on in daylight.

“What’s your name, son?”

Everett looked at Mother Serene.

“He may answer,” she said.

“Everett.”

“Everett.” The man repeated it with a public friendliness that did not reach the rest of him. “You help with the water?”

“Yes, sir.”

“That’s good. Everybody’s got to pitch in when things get tight.”

Everett did not answer because the sentence had not contained a question. The man seemed to expect some small grateful noise anyway. When it did not come, he glanced at Mother Serene, and she smiled for him. Everett saw the exchange and understood that the county man preferred children who completed his idea of kindness. That made him ordinary. Ordinary people were not harmless. They were only predictable by different instruments.

They crossed to the east cistern through heat that smelled of dust, iron, and the faint meat sweetness from the smokehouse. The cistern sat behind the infirmary under a framed shade roof, its access hatch bolted and tagged, its concrete apron hairline-cracked from winter heave. Two girls were hauling laundry tubs nearby. They did not look up. Neither did the boys sorting scrap by the fence. The settlement made its innocence available to inspection in layers: laboring children, clean yards, tools stored squarely, scripture boards facing the lane, no weapon visible except the old single-shot rifle over the smokehouse door, which the Sisters had left there for coyotes and county comfort.

The county man knelt and peered at the gauge as Chauncey lifted the hatch. The smell that rose from inside was mineral and cold. He made notes, asked questions about draw rates, household count, graywater, livestock. Mother Serene answered with the mild authority of someone reciting facts that had already submitted to her. Some of the numbers were true. Some were shaped. Everett knew which, because he had copied the storage logs in Mother Ester’s office until his fingers cramped. The county wanted numbers that could fit boxes. Mother Serene gave them boxes with numbers inside. This was not lying in the way Sister Agnes meant when she struck little Naomi for saying she had washed both hands when only one was clean. This was governance.

The man asked about the private spring east of the road.

Chauncey’s rag stopped moving.

Mother Serene’s smile did not change. “That spring is seasonal.”

“County map has it marked perennial.”

“The county map also has the old quarry road marked passable.”

He laughed because he thought she was joking with him. She let him have the laugh. Then he said, “Still, I’ll need to take a look. If there’s a viable source and you’re drawing from an unpermitted point, that changes the remediation plan.”

Everett did not know what remediation meant in the county sense. In New Mercy, correction had plain words: fast, prayer, silence, labor, confinement. County words were padded, built to travel through hallways without bruising anyone who carried them. But he understood the shape of the problem. The spring was not seasonal. The spring fed the hidden tank behind the lower ridge, and the hidden tank fed what was not on any map: the women’s house beyond the windbreak, the buried rooms, the second storage barn with no windows, the place where Mother Ester kept records wrapped in oilcloth and where Everett had once seen a radio set better than the one in the shed.

Mother Serene turned her head slightly. The brim of her gray bonnet threw a clean shadow over one cheek. “Everett,” she said, “show Mr. Voss the wash road after supper. Chauncey will be occupied with the pump.”

Mr. Voss opened his mouth, probably to say he could find it himself, but Mother Serene had already made the arrangement sound like hospitality, and hospitality trapped him more neatly than refusal would have. He smiled again. The sun flashed on the metal clip of his pen.

“Yes, ma’am,” Everett said.

He spent the rest of the afternoon at ordinary work. He hauled water from the cistern to the kitchen. He carried kindling. He sat with the younger boys through doctrine recitation and watched a fly walk along the inside of the window glass, stopping every few inches to clean its front legs with obscene delicacy. Sister Miriam spoke of obedience as a vessel. Everett repeated the verses with the others and kept his voice level. His mind did not race. Racing was for people surprised by their own lives. He had been told the necessary thing before the necessary thing had a name.

At supper, Mr. Voss ate beans, bread, and preserved peaches at the visitor table beneath the framed county certificate authorizing New Mercy’s school exemption. He praised the bread. He praised the orderliness of the children. He said it was refreshing to see young people raised with standards. Mother Ester, broad-faced and dry-eyed, nodded as if he had correctly identified a species of bird. Everett sat three tables away and watched the man’s hands. They were soft but not useless. There was a small scar near the thumb of the right hand, white against the pink skin. Wedding band. Cheap watch. Truck keys clipped to belt. He wiped peach syrup from his beard with his napkin and missed a place near the corner of his mouth.

After supper, the light went long and coppery. The heat came off the yard in shallow waves. Everett walked ahead of Mr. Voss down the lane, past the schoolhouse, past the last wire fence, toward the place where the county road sank between low bluffs and the wash cut across in a bed of pale stones. Grasshoppers sprang from the weeds at their feet. Somewhere behind them, the chapel bell rang once for evening work period. Everett did not look back. Looking back implied expectation.

“You folks always this quiet?” Mr. Voss asked.

Everett considered whether the question required an answer. “No, sir.”

“No?”

“Sometimes it’s windy.”

The man gave a surprised bark of laughter. “That a joke?”

Everett kept walking.

The wash road was not a road in the county sense. It was two ruts that appeared and vanished depending on season, flood, and the willingness of whoever was driving to accept responsibility for the underside of a vehicle. The bluffs on either side were low enough to climb and high enough to hide what happened below them from the settlement. A culvert mouth showed black under one bank, half-choked with tumbleweed and silt. In spring, water sometimes ran there hard enough to move stones the size of a fist. Now the bed was dry, or nearly dry. The last storm had left only damp patches under the deeper shade.

Mr. Voss stopped to write something. “This isn’t on the map.”

“No, sir.”

“It connect to the east draw?”

“Yes, sir.”

“You know, Everett, you don’t have to call me sir every time.”

Everett looked at him then. The man was smiling with one corner of his mouth, trying to create a private treaty. The sky behind him had begun to purple. A line of birds crossed high over the ridge, too far away to identify. Everett felt, with a clarity that did not feel like feeling, the smallness of Mr. Voss’s mercy. The man wanted to be the good kind of authority. He wanted the child to know it. He might even be troubled by what he saw here. He might write a report that caused other men to arrive with questions and forms and trucks and maybe, if the report climbed high enough, warrants. He might save someone later without ever learning a name. He might also drink from his plastic bottle while girls pulled the dry well until their hands blistered. Both could be true. Adults were often several people stacked badly inside one coat.

Everett said, “Yes, sir.”

The man sighed and looked down at his clipboard.

By the time they reached the bend, the settlement had disappeared behind the bluff. The wind moved through rabbitbrush with a dry whispering sound. Everett stopped at the place Mother Serene had indicated three days earlier without pointing. He had walked this route twice since then. He knew where the stones shifted underfoot, where the bank crumbled, where sound flattened because the wash widened briefly before narrowing again under the old cattle bridge. He knew where Chauncey had left the thing wrapped in canvas beneath the rusted sheet metal. He did not look toward it. Looking was for amateurs and penitent men.

Mr. Voss said, “How much farther?”

“Not far.”

“Figures.” He capped his pen and wiped sweat from his neck. “Everything out here is not far until it is.”

That was almost wise. Everett wished, briefly and without softness, that the man had not said anything almost wise. It made the next part less clean.

The wind shifted. From far behind them came the faint sound of the chapel bell again, two strikes this time, then silence. The signal was unnecessary. Mother Serene trusted him. Mother Ester trusted function, not people. God, Everett had been told, trusted obedience after it had been proven. He waited for the interior pressure he had been taught to call grace, the descending certainty that made the body an instrument and the world a narrow corridor. It came, but thinner than usual. Around it was the evening, the dust, the pink county man with peach syrup dried at his mouth, the hidden spring beyond the ridge, the little girls at the pump, Chauncey’s stopped rag, and the laminated notice curling on the kitchen door.

Mr. Voss turned back toward him.

Everett did the necessary thing.

Later, walking home alone under the first stars, he carried the clipboard against his ribs inside his shirt so the paper would not show if someone came down the lane. His hands were clean because Mother Serene had planned for water where the county had not. The wash behind him had resumed being landscape. A truck passed on the county road far to the west, headlights moving slowly behind dust like two careful eyes. Everett stopped until they were gone. He expected, as he sometimes did after obedience, for the sky to open with approval or punishment, for some visible confirmation that the world had noticed the difference between what had been alive and what had been made useful. Nothing happened. The steppe kept its own counsel. The settlement lights glowed low ahead of him, shielded and orderly, each one placed where a rule required it.

Mother Serene waited by the washhouse.

“You were seen by no one?”

“No, Mother.”

“You brought the notes?”

He gave her the clipboard. She did not praise him. Praise was for children who needed to be reminded of the transaction. She looked toward the dark line of the road, then back at him, and for one suspended second Everett thought she might touch his face. She did not.

“Wash,” she said. “Then sleep.”

“Yes, Mother.”

He went to the pump. The handle caught twice before water came. He worked it with slow, even pressure, listening to the iron complain down in the well, and when the water spilled at last into the basin it looked black under the stars. He washed until his hands were cold. He washed past usefulness, past instruction, past the place where guilt might have been if guilt had been one of the permitted names. Across the yard, the laminated notice lifted and tapped against the kitchen door in the wind, a small official sound, patient and meaningless and impossible to silence completely.
