# Everett — County Hours

The county building had once been a bank, and it had kept the habits of money after the money left. The lobby ceiling rose higher than a county office required, stained in pale squares where some older ornament had been removed and never replaced. Marble climbed halfway up the walls before giving way to beige paint. The floor still held the faint grid of teller windows, ghost-lines in the tile where counters had been pried out and where people now formed lines for vehicle registration, property tax disputes, probation appointments, records requests, child support hearings, water permits, and all the other petitions by which a life asked the state to acknowledge it had happened. Everett sat beneath a brass clock that did not work and listened to the building move paper through itself.

He had been there since morning. Morning had been a separate climate. Men in coats had brought him through a side entrance before the public counters opened, one hand at his elbow though he was not fighting, one hand hovering near the place on the man’s belt where authority kept its heavier answer. They had not handcuffed him in the lobby. That decision had been discussed in the parking lot in lowered voices while exhaust drifted from three idling vehicles and dawn came weakly over the courthouse roof. Juvenile status. Flight risk. Media risk. Medical risk. One of them had said cult kid as if it were a diagnosis and another had said alleged, not because he cared about precision but because the word protected the room. Everett had stood between them with a paper cup of burnt coffee warming his fingers. He had not been given the coffee to drink. A woman from emergency services had put it in his hands because he was shivering and because the blanket around his shoulders made everyone look at him too directly.

By ten, the blanket had been taken away and replaced with a gray sweatshirt from lost property. By eleven, two different women had asked whether he wanted a sandwich. By noon, someone had decided he should not be given plastic utensils. By one, the sandwich had appeared anyway, cut diagonally, wrapped in cellophane, with an apple and a carton of milk on a tray. Everett ate half because refusing food made adults suspicious and eating all of it made them relieved. Neither response was useful. The apple was red wax over meal. The milk tasted like cardboard. He placed the unopened half of the sandwich back in its wrapper and folded the cellophane along its existing creases.

Across from him, a framed photograph of the county commissioners hung above a rack of pamphlets. Six men and one woman stood on the steps of this same building in better weather, smiling with the strained civic optimism of people who had agreed on drainage funding. Behind them, the courthouse dome rose white against a blue sky. Everett studied their shoes. Two pairs polished, one scuffed, one woman’s heel caught slightly in a gap between stones. There were systems everywhere once a person learned to see them. Not conspiracies, which the Mothers loved because conspiracies made the world intimate and chosen. Systems were colder. A person could pass through a system and be changed by it without anyone in the room hating him enough to make the change memorable.

“Everett?”

He looked up.

The woman standing in front of him had introduced herself three times. Tina Alvarez, Department of Human Services. She was short, broad through the hips, with hair pinned at the nape of her neck and a soft brown cardigan that had collected lint along one sleeve. She carried a file too thick for someone who had been in county custody less than a day. That meant the file contained other people’s versions of him: sheriff’s report, emergency intake, names from New Mercy, photographs of buildings, maybe the first pages taken from Mother Ester’s office before the smoke made the papers brittle at the edges. He wondered whether anyone had found the buried cabinet beneath the women’s house. He wondered whether Mother Serene had burned it before she ran.

Tina sat in the chair angled near his, not directly opposite. Adults thought side angles made them less threatening. Sometimes they were right.

“How are you feeling?” she asked.

He considered a truthful answer and found it too large to be useful. “Fine.”

“Any pain?”

“No.”

Her eyes moved once to his left hand. The knuckles were split. There was a burn along the inside of his wrist, treated and bandaged, white gauze bright against his skin. At the collar of the borrowed sweatshirt, a line of tattooed color rose and disappeared again, too vivid for the room. The intake nurse had stared while pretending not to. Tina did not pretend. She saw it, recorded it somewhere behind her eyes, and returned to his face.

“I’m going to explain what happens next,” she said. “Some of it may change. I don’t want to lie to you.”

That was unusual enough that he looked at her properly.

“You’re not going back to New Mercy,” she said.

He waited for the force behind the sentence. There was none. The building did not shift. The dead clock remained at 4:17. A man at the vehicle counter argued that his trailer weight had been entered wrong and that he should not owe the additional fee. Somewhere beyond the lobby, a copier jammed and began to beep in patient accusation.

Tina said, “There are criminal matters. There are also dependency matters. They are separate processes, even when they involve the same facts. You are a minor. That matters. It does not make everything simple. But it matters.”

Everett said nothing.

“You’ll meet with an attorney. Not today, probably. Today we need medical clearance, a safer placement, and another interview if your attorney permits it. You can decline to speak until then. Do you understand?”

He understood the words individually. Together they formed a structure that did not resemble judgment as he had been raised to expect it. The Mothers judged by proximity. They entered the room. They looked at you. The judgment became weather. County judgment moved by appointment, jurisdiction, statute, docket, signature. It could be postponed. It could be misfiled. It could be appealed by adults who had never washed blood from a pump handle under stars.

“I killed people,” he said.

Tina did not flinch, which was to her credit. The man at the vehicle counter lowered his voice by accident and then raised it again. Tina folded both hands over the file. Her nails were short, unpainted, one cuticle torn raw.

“I know that’s part of what we need to talk about,” she said.

“No,” Everett said. “Not part.”

Her mouth tightened. Not fear. Not disgust exactly. A disciplined grief, maybe. Something working inside rules. “Everett, there are things I can’t ask you without counsel present.”

“Then why are you here?”

“To keep you alive until the rules catch up.”

He almost smiled. It would have been inappropriate, which was not the same as wrong. “Is that your job?”

“Some days.”

He looked back at the commissioners. The woman’s heel remained trapped in the stone. “What happens if the rules don’t catch up?”

“They usually do. Badly, sometimes. Late. But they do.”

He believed she believed this. That interested him more than the statement itself. Belief in New Mercy had been vertical. It descended from Mothers to Sisters to children, from scripture boards to chores, from doctrine into the body. Tina’s belief was lateral, held together by offices, phones, other tired women, court calendars, safe houses, budget lines, cars with mileage reimbursement forms in their glove compartments. It was not pure. It was not strong in the way Mother Serene was strong. But it had reach.

A deputy came through the lobby with a paper bag of evidence sealed in red tape. Everett recognized his own shirt inside the bag. It had been blue once and then brown in places and then black where the fire had touched it. The deputy glanced toward him, away, then toward him again. Men kept doing that. Everett’s face did not match what they wanted from the reports. He had killed too much to be a child and was too plainly a child to be the thing they could hate without complication.

Tina followed his eyes. “The news trucks are still outside,” she said. “We’ll move you through the basement when it’s time.”

“Why?”

“Because people want a story.”

“They have one.”

“They want one they can understand quickly.”

He looked at her again. She had said it without contempt, which made it truer. “And you?”

“I want you to have lunch, see a doctor, and meet the woman who might take you for a few nights.”

“Might.”

“It’s not a simple placement.”

“No.”

Tina exhaled through her nose. “She knows enough to say yes carefully. That’s better than someone saying yes because they think kindness is the same thing as competence.”

Everett let that sit. The county building hummed around them. Doors opened and closed. A child cried near the records counter and was hushed by a man who sounded more embarrassed than angry. A clerk stamped forms with a heavy mechanical rhythm: received, received, received. Everett found himself counting the stamps until Tina spoke again.

“There’s another thing,” she said. “Some of the people from New Mercy are missing.”

He already knew that. The absence had shape. Mother Serene would not be found unless she chose a version of being found that benefited her. Mother Ester, if alive, would become paper. She would move through names, church kitchens, widow rooms, sympathetic landowners, storage lockers, old debts. The Sisters had always practiced dispersal as if it were liturgy. Everett had burned three buildings and opened two locked rooms and left by the lower ridge expecting God to strike him before dawn. He had not believed he would succeed. He had believed rebellion would reveal the hidden mechanism of the universe and that the Mothers, corrected by heaven, would become terrible and luminous and right after all.

Instead, the women had run.

Instead, the county had found him vomiting smoke beside an irrigation ditch with a stolen revolver empty in his hand and a girl from the lower dormitory clinging to his coat. Instead, the sky had remained indifferent. Instead, dawn had come and revealed the settlement not as a holy body but as buildings with bad wiring, hidden rooms, ledgers, dehydrated children, stockpiled antibiotics, diesel cans, and bones in places where bones should not have been.

“Everett,” Tina said softly.

He realized he had closed his eyes.

“I’m not asleep.”

“I know.”

He opened them. “If Mother Serene is missing, you should check the county water hearings.”

Tina went still.

“She kept names,” he said. “Not important names. Useful names. Men who decide where inspections happen. Men who know which roads are graded and which aren’t. Churches that share vans. A dentist in Pueblo. A woman who works seasonal hiring at the fruit plant. The Mothers liked people who controlled gates and did not think of themselves as powerful.”

Tina’s hand moved to the file, then stopped. “Do you want to write those down?”

“No.”

“Can you say why?”

“Because if I write them down, you will put the paper in the file.”

“Yes.”

“And then everyone who touches the file becomes part of the route.”

For the first time, Tina looked tired in a way that had nothing to do with hours. “That may be true,” she said. “It may also be the only way to get anyone to act.”

Everett thought of the buried cabinet. Oilcloth. Ledgers. Mother Ester’s hands turning pages with a reverence she never gave scripture because scripture did not need her protection. “Then give me a map.”

“I can’t just—”

“A county road map. Not state. County. The one with maintenance grades.”

“Why?”

“Because she won’t hide where no one can reach her. She’ll hide where reaching her requires permission.”

Tina stared at him. Around them, ordinary government continued at its ordinary speed. The man with the trailer left angry. A clerk called number forty-two. The brass clock remained wrong above Everett’s head, but everyone still moved as if time existed somewhere else and could be consulted by those with access to better instruments.

Finally Tina stood. “I’ll see what I can do.”

“Ask Chauncey about culverts.”

“Chauncey is in surgery.”

Everett had not known that. For a moment, the lobby lost definition. The marble, the pamphlets, the commissioners, the seal on the floor, all of it thinned around a single practical fact. Chauncey had been alive when Everett left him. Hurt, but alive. Chauncey had opened the lower gate. Chauncey had sworn at the latch for sticking. Chauncey had said, not looking at him, if you’re doing it, do it before the wind shifts. Everett had not thanked him because gratitude would have made the action smaller.

“Will he live?” Everett asked.

“They think so.”

He nodded once. There was no room to put what that meant. Not here. Perhaps nowhere.

Tina left and returned after eighteen minutes with a folded county road map, a yellow legal pad, and a deputy who clearly disliked the arrangement but had been outvoted by someone not present. Everett took the map and spread it on the chair beside him. The county appeared in lines and numbers, section roads, state routes, dry creek names, school district boundaries, reservoirs, maintenance sheds, seasonal closures, cattle guards, bridges with load ratings. New Mercy was not marked. The land where it stood was shown as vacant ranch parcel under a holding company name Mother Ester had made the older boys practice saying without amusement.

Everett placed his finger east of the settlement, where the map showed an unmaintained track ending at nothing. “Here,” he said. “There’s a gate hidden behind a windbreak. The lock is county surplus. Not hard to open. Past it, the road splits. Left goes to the spring. Right goes to the women’s house. There’s a buried tank here. If the ground sounds hollow, don’t let anyone stand too close. It was poured badly.”

Tina wrote. The deputy watched. Everett moved his finger again.

“Here. If Mother Ester is alive, she won’t take the highway. She’ll go south along ranch tracks until the old quarry. There’s a trailer near the quarry with no plates and a propane tank painted green. It belongs to a man named Caleb, unless he changed it. He owes Mother Serene.”

“What does he owe?” Tina asked.

Everett looked at the map. “His son.”

The deputy muttered something under his breath. Tina did not look away from Everett. “Go on.”

So he did. He gave them roads first, because roads were safer than names. He gave them gates, tanks, sheds, abandoned wells, two churches, one private school, a warehouse that accepted deliveries under three business names, a dentist who had pulled teeth without questions and had liked to pray with one hand on a child’s forehead while the anesthetic was still working. He did not give them everything. Not because he remained loyal. Loyalty had burned out of him in a series of rooms. He held back because information moved differently once surrendered. It became evidence, and evidence belonged to whoever could sign for it. He had been used as an instrument long enough to respect the danger of tools.

When he finished, the light in the lobby had changed. Afternoon had become the kind of late afternoon that made the courthouse windows look briefly golden and almost kind. Tina’s legal pad was filled with her square, rapid handwriting. The deputy had stopped pretending not to be shaken. Everett folded the map along its creases. His hands did not tremble until the last fold, and then only slightly.

Tina noticed. She noticed too much. “You did well,” she said.

“No.”

“Okay,” she said after a moment. “You did something useful.”

That he could accept.

A door opened near the basement stairs. Another woman entered, tall and narrow-shouldered, with black boots, a denim jacket, and hair dyed a dark red that the fluorescent lights treated badly. She carried a paper grocery bag against one hip. She looked less like a foster placement than someone who had arrived to argue with a landlord. There were silver rings in one ear and a faded tattoo disappearing under her sleeve. Her face, though, was open in a way Everett immediately distrusted. Sweetness, in his experience, was either ignorance or bait.

Tina stood. “Madeline.”

The woman smiled, nervous and warm. “Hi.”

Everett looked at the grocery bag. It contained, from the smell, oranges, bread, and something cinnamon.

Madeline saw him looking and lifted the bag slightly. “I didn’t know what you liked,” she said. “So I made too many guesses.”

No Mother had ever said a thing like that. Everett searched it for the hidden command and did not find one quickly enough to answer.

Tina touched the back of the chair, grounding the room with one hand. “Everett, this is Madeline Ross. She has been cleared for emergency placement while we work through the next steps.”

Madeline did not step closer. Good. Either instinct or training. She looked at his face, then at the map, then back at him. Her eyes were gray-green and more tired than her voice. “My house is boring,” she said. “I should warn you up front. There’s a porch light that makes a clicking sound, and the neighbor’s dog thinks mailboxes are communists.”

The deputy blinked.

Everett said, “What?”

Madeline’s smile faltered, then recovered softly. “Yeah, that one usually lands better with adults. Sorry.”

Something moved in Everett’s chest, not amusement exactly, but near it and therefore dangerous. He looked down at the folded map. The county building held him in its old bank bones. Behind the walls, offices sorted guilt from dependency, crime from survival, statement from evidence, child from weapon, as if the distinctions had been waiting all along in labeled drawers. Outside, news trucks idled for the version of him they could use by evening. Somewhere south, Mother Serene was either running or listening. Somewhere in the hospital, Chauncey was deciding whether to remain alive.

Everett stood.

Madeline’s gaze dropped once to his hands, then rose again. She did not offer to carry the map. She did not touch him. She said, “Car’s out back. Tina said you might want the quiet way.”

“Yes,” Everett said.

As they walked toward the basement stairs, the brass clock remained at 4:17 above the lobby, keeping its private ruined time while the county continued without it.
