# Everett — The Night Room

Everett woke with his hand around Madeline’s throat.

Not tightly. That was the first fact his mind chose because it was the only one with immediate use. His thumb was set wrong for pressure. His palm had not seated under the jaw. The fingers of his right hand lay along the side of her neck, touching skin and tendon, feeling the pulse there move once, twice, alive and fast beneath the surface. His left wrist throbbed against the bedspread where he had twisted it beneath him. He was not standing over her. He was sitting upright on the blue plaid bed in the upstairs room she had called his, shoes still on, lamp still burning, the house dark beyond the open door. Madeline crouched beside the bed with both hands raised. Not high. Not surrendering theatrically. One hand had stopped near his wrist without touching him. The other hovered beside her own shoulder, useless and open, as if she had begun to defend herself and then lost faith in every motion available to her. Her eyes were wide, gray-green in the bad yellow light, and her face had gone very still.

Everett let go.

Madeline moved back only after he did. She did not scramble. She slid one knee along the rug, caught the bedframe with her fingertips, and lowered herself to the floor near the doorway. Her breathing came hard through her nose. The marks on her neck had not yet become bruises. They were only red places where his fingers had been, too light to seem important and too exact to be anything else. Downstairs, the porch light clicked through the window glass with its small defective sound. Nine seconds. Click. The refrigerator hummed below. Outside, a car moved over wet pavement, though he had not heard rain.

“Back farther,” Everett said.

His voice sounded thin. Younger than he permitted it to be in daylight.

Madeline pushed herself another foot away. “Here?”

“Yes.”

He put both hands flat on his own knees. He looked at them because looking at her throat made the room tilt. The right hand looked ordinary again. Hands were terrible that way. They resumed being useful immediately.

“Can you breathe?” he asked.

“Yes.”

“Swallow.”

She swallowed. The motion passed beneath the place his fingers had been.

“Pain?”

“A little.”

“Where?”

She touched the side of her neck, then lowered her hand as if touching it had made the injury more real. “Here.”

“Not the middle?”

“No.”

“Any dizziness?”

“No.”

“Voice sounds normal.”

“I think so.”

He nodded once. Not reassurance. Inventory.

Madeline stayed on the rug with one knee bent under her and the other boot half unlaced. She had put the boots on without socks. One bare ankle showed white in the lamplight. She must have come upstairs quickly. Too quickly. He had not heard her. He had not heard the door open. There were too many failures in that sequence to count all at once.

“Why are you in here?” he asked.

“You were screaming.”

“No.”

“You were.”

“I don’t scream.”

“You did tonight.”

The correction should have angered him. It did not. She sounded frightened, and because she was frightened, she was less likely to be making the room kinder than it was.

“What did I say?”

“Nothing I understood.”

“Names?”

“I don’t know.”

“Don’t guess.”

“I’m not.”

He looked past her to the open door. The chair he had wedged beneath the knob had been moved aside but not overturned. The lock had been turned. His arrangement against entry had been taken apart quietly enough that he had not surfaced until his hand was already on her neck.

“You opened the door.”

“Yes.”

“With what?”

“A screwdriver.”

“Where was it?”

“Kitchen drawer.”

“You brought it up here.”

“Yes.”

“Before I stopped making noise?”

Madeline blinked. “What?”

“You got the screwdriver before you knew I was quiet.”

“Yes.”

“So you were going to open it anyway.”

“If you didn’t answer.”

“I didn’t answer.”

“No.”

He looked at the knob. Cheap brass, scratched around the emergency hole. It had never been much of a lock. He had known that and still needed it to mean something.

“You knew you could open it,” he said.

Madeline’s mouth tightened. “Yes.”

“Before tonight.”

“Yes.”

“Why didn’t you tell me?”

“I should have.”

“That is not an answer.”

She looked down, then back up. “Because I didn’t want to stand in the hall and explain to you that the lock I said was yours wasn’t really yours. Not on the first night.”

That answer made sense. It did not help. Sensible harm was still harm.

“But it wasn’t,” he said.

“No.”

“You said it was.”

“I know.”

“You lied.”

Madeline flinched once, not dramatically. “I thought I was giving you privacy.”

“You were giving me a door you could open.”

“Yes.”

She did not argue the point. That made him trust her less for a moment, then more, then not at all. The order changed too quickly to use.

On the floor near the baseboard, the spiral notebook lay bent under its own cover. The pen had rolled beneath the chair. He did not remember throwing either one. He remembered the basement corridor beneath the women’s house. Lime dust at the edges of the floor. Battery lanterns making everyone’s skin look waxy. A metal door whose hinges had been painted too many times. Mother Ester’s shoes visible first, the rest of her body arranged above them with the dry patience of office work. A girl on the other side of the door trying to breathe quietly enough to remain a person and not become instruction.

That had happened when he was eleven. Or twelve. The ages had loosened after the fire. They came back out of order, like papers from a file dropped in wind.

Madeline shifted her weight.

Everett’s head snapped toward her.

She stopped. “My leg is going numb.”

“Say before you move.”

“I just did.”

“Say what you are doing.”

“I’m moving my foot. Not standing up. Just my foot.”

He watched. She moved slowly, dragging the boot an inch across the rug. The floorboard did not creak this time.

“Okay?” she asked.

“No.”

She nodded as if that, too, was information.

The porch light clicked. Nine seconds. The house took a small breath around them.

“You need to call Marian,” he said.

“I will.”

“Now.”

“In a minute.”

“No.”

Madeline’s face tightened. Fear had made her careful. Pain made her less so. “I need one minute, Everett.”

“Don’t use my name like that.”

“Like what?”

“Like a leash.”

She closed her mouth.

He could see her wanting to explain herself. He could see her decide not to. That was better than apology. Apologies asked to be managed.

“I need one minute,” she said again, without his name.

“Why?”

“Because if I call while I’m shaking, I’ll sound worse than I am.”

“You are worse than you think.”

“That may be true.”

“You could have died.”

“I know.”

“You don’t know.”

“No,” she said. “Not like you mean it.”

That was the first thing she had said since he woke that did not ask to be corrected.

He looked at her throat. The marks had begun to darken at the edges. He wanted, with simple practical violence, to disable his own hand. The wish did not arrive as grief. It arrived as maintenance. A damaged tool should be taken apart before it failed in public. He flexed his fingers to measure how close the thought was to action. The hand obeyed him. It remained attached. It looked human. That seemed like poor design.

“Tell her exactly,” he said.

“I will.”

“Tell her you opened the door.”

“Yes.”

“With a screwdriver.”

“Yes.”

“Tell her I had my hand on your throat.”

Madeline swallowed again. “Yes.”

“Tell her you don’t know how hard.”

“I don’t.”

“Say that.”

“I will.”

“Tell her I told you to call.”

“Why?”

“Because if I wanted it hidden, I wouldn’t.”

Madeline looked at him for a long second. “All right.”

“Not because I’m good.”

“I didn’t say that.”

“Don’t think it either.”

Her expression changed then, not softening exactly, but losing some of its attempt at steadiness. “I’m not having big thoughts right now.”

That also made sense.

He looked at the hall behind her. The moon-shaped night-light at the top of the stairs glowed blue and stupid near the baseboard. The hallway was not a room. It had directions. A person sitting in a hall could see approach and retreat at once. That did not make it safe. It made it less false.

“I can’t stay in here,” he said.

Madeline looked toward the bed, then the door, then him. “Where do you want to go?”

“Don’t ask wants.”

“Where can you go?”

“The hall.”

“Okay.”

“Don’t say okay.”

She rubbed both hands once over her face, hard enough to pull at the skin, then dropped them. The motion was too tired to be theatrical. “I heard you.”

He moved to the edge of the bed. Madeline shifted away before he stood. He saw the preparation and hated the small usefulness of it. Fear made adults legible. Legible was not the same as trustworthy, but it reduced the number of surprises. He stood. The room tilted briefly. His shoes left dry grit on the bedspread where he had slept.

Madeline saw the dirt and said nothing about it.

He walked to the doorway and sat on the hallway floor with his back against the wall opposite her, knees bent, hands laid open on his thighs. From there he could see the bedroom, the stairs, the top of the linen closet, and Madeline’s throat without turning his head. The night-light threw blue over the toe of his right shoe.

Madeline stayed near the doorframe, not blocking the stairs.

For a while neither of them spoke.

The refrigerator below shut off. Water moved somewhere in the pipes with the faint shudder of old pressure finding a joint. Public water, Everett thought. Treated, pumped, billed, governed. The house possessed systems that announced themselves mainly by failing. The porch light clicked again over the front door, counting a piece of time no one had requested.

“At New Mercy,” Everett said, “they wrote down noises at night.”

Madeline did not answer quickly. “From the children?”

“Yes.”

“What kind of noises?”

“Talking. Crying. Getting up. Names said in sleep.”

She pulled her sleeves down over her hands. “Why?”

“To know what people hid when they were awake.”

Madeline looked at the wall across from her. She did not say sorry. Good. Sorry would have required him to dispose of it.

“If I said anything,” he said, “you know now.”

“I told you. I didn’t understand it.”

“You know there was something to hear.”

“I already knew that.”

“No.”

“I did,” she said, and there was enough tired irritation in the answer that it became more believable. “You came out of a place with hidden rooms and dead people and a county file nobody wants to carry under one arm. I didn’t need a noise at night to know there was something.”

He stared at her.

Madeline looked back, scared and irritated and still there.

“That was too much,” she said after a moment.

“Yes.”

“Still true.”

“Yes.”

Neither of them seemed to know what to do with the agreement.

She touched her neck again and winced.

“Don’t rub it,” Everett said.

“I forgot.”

“You shouldn’t.”

“I’m new to being strangled awake.”

He looked at her.

“That was bad,” she said.

“Yes.”

“Sorry.”

“Don’t apologize to me for that.”

“Right.” She pressed her lips together. “Right.”

The silence after was not comfortable. It did not need to be. It was at least not dishonest.

Madeline pushed herself carefully to her feet. This time she narrated the motion without being asked. “I’m standing. Going downstairs. Calling Marian. Normal voice.”

“Not whispering.”

“Not whispering.”

“Not loud.”

“Not loud.”

He nodded once.

She went downstairs.

Everett listened to each step. The house announced her descent in groans and settling boards. In the kitchen, a drawer opened. The phone was lifted from the counter. Buttons clicked under her fingers. Then her voice rose through the floor at ordinary volume, not hidden, not offered to him either.

“Marian. It’s Madeline. Wake up.”

A pause.

“No, actually sit up first.”

Another pause, longer. Everett imagined Marian in whatever room she slept in, reaching for glasses, paper, the version of herself that belonged to emergency.

“There was an incident,” Madeline said. “He was asleep when it started.”

Everett picked up the notebook from the rug and smoothed the bent page with his palm. The paper held a faint dent where the pen had struck through from some pressure during the night. Not writing. Almost writing. He found the pen beneath the chair and set it across the notebook.

His hand shook once.

He watched it until it stopped.

Downstairs, Madeline said, “I opened the bedroom door. With a screwdriver. Yes. I know. I’m telling you that part first.”

The floorboards carried Marian’s reply only as a low pulse.

“I thought he was hurt.”

Another pulse.

“No. Listen. When I got in, his hand was on my throat.”

The sentence entered the room through the floorboards and settled there with the lamp and the bed and the shoes he had not removed. It sounded worse spoken aloud. It sounded accurate. Accuracy had no kindness in it, but it had edges a person could hold without being deceived.

A long silence followed.

“No,” Madeline said. “He let go when he woke up.”

Another silence.

“I don’t know how long. I don’t know how hard.”

Everett put the pen to the top line. Doctrine had trained his hand toward sentences that began with I have failed or I submit or I understand my correction. He wrote none of those.

He wrote: The room changes after sleep.

The sentence looked strange in his handwriting. Less like evidence than observation. He stared at it until the words stopped trying to become a weapon.

Downstairs, Madeline said, “He told me to call you.”

The answer on the other end rose enough that he heard the shape of alarm, if not the words.

“No lights,” Madeline said. “Do not send a cruiser with lights to my street unless you want every curtain in the county involved.”

Marian spoke again.

“I’m not refusing help. I’m asking you not to turn the porch into a show.”

Everett wrote beneath the first sentence: A lock only works in one direction.

He did not know if that was true. It seemed true enough for the room. A lock kept out what remained outside. It did nothing for what came with the body, with sleep, with the trained hand returning from rooms that should have burned completely and had not.

The call continued. Time of incident. Injuries. Whether Madeline could breathe. Whether there were weapons in the room. Whether Everett had threatened her after waking. Whether he was currently agitated. Whether she felt safe waiting until Marian arrived. The questions moved upward through plaster and joists and old domestic dust. Everett arranged each answer into the file it would become. The file would become a call note, the note would become an incident report, the incident report would become a fact in the morning hearing, and the fact would sit among other facts wearing no sign to show which ones had been born from sleep.

At the end Madeline said, “Bring coffee if you have it.”

A pause.

“No, I’m not kidding. You’re bad without it.”

She hung up.

The house changed after the call, not visibly, but in the way a room changed when someone had been sent for. Everett remained on the hallway floor with the notebook in his lap. Madeline stayed downstairs longer than necessary. He heard water run. A cabinet open. The scrape of a chair at the kitchen table, then no movement for a while. He did not imagine her crying. He did not permit himself to imagine it. Adults were entitled to their own rooms when they had them.

A car door shut outside at 6:31. Not a slam. Marian, probably. Or a deputy told not to frighten the neighbors and therefore making every movement resemble secrecy. Walter began barking before the knock came. He barked with renewed ideological conviction, hurling himself against whatever morning government had approached the street. Madeline crossed to the front door. The porch light clicked above her, faithful to its defect.

Everett stood and went to the top of the stairs.

From there he could see the front hall, the kitchen doorway, Madeline’s untied boots, her hand on the knob. She looked up at him before opening the door. The mark on her throat had darkened slightly, no longer red but beginning to blue at the edges. He forced himself to look at it and not away.

“I wrote on the page,” he said.

Madeline’s face changed as if he had handed her an object too fragile to name. “Do you want to keep it?”

“Yes.”

“Then keep it.”

“You won’t ask to read it?”

“No.”

He did not trust the permanence of that answer. He trusted that she meant it while saying it, which was different and perhaps the only kind of trust available before breakfast.

She opened the door.

Morning entered first, gray and wet and smelling faintly of asphalt. Marian stood on the porch in yesterday’s cardigan under a raincoat, hair pinned badly, a paper cup of coffee in one hand and a county phone in the other. Behind her, at the curb, a deputy leaned against an unmarked sedan with both arms folded, trying to look like a man present by coincidence. He failed.

Marian looked at Madeline’s throat. Then at Everett on the stairs. She did not soften her face for him. She did not harden it either. That restraint seemed to cost her something.

“Stay there for a second,” Marian said.

Everett stopped with one hand on the banister.

Madeline remained beside the door. “Say it to him.”

Marian looked at her.

“He can hear you,” Madeline said.

Marian turned back to the stairs. “Everett, stay where you are until I’m inside.”

“Is the deputy coming in?”

“No.”

“Is his radio on?”

Marian looked over her shoulder. “Tom, turn it down.”

The deputy said something Everett could not make out.

“Now,” Marian said.

A short burst of static came from outside and then stopped.

Madeline stepped back to let Marian enter. Marian wiped her shoes on the mat before crossing the threshold, not because the mat mattered but because some part of her still belonged to ordinary houses. The gesture made Everett dislike her less.

“Kitchen,” Marian said. “Door open. Hands visible. Nobody behind anybody.”

Everett descended.

Each stair gave under his weight with its own small complaint. Madeline moved aside to give him the hall. Marian remained near the threshold until he reached the bottom, which told him she had thought about distances before knocking. The porch light clicked above all of them. Nine seconds. Click. The sound marked the space between instruction and obedience, between danger and care, between a house and the agencies that could enter it.

In the kitchen, the cinnamon rolls remained untouched in foil and the coffee had gone bitter in the pot. Everett sat where he had sat the night before. Marian took the chair nearest the door. Madeline stayed standing for a moment, then seemed to remember she lived there and sat too. No one prayed. No one called the nightmare by another name. The porch light continued to click over the threshold, cheap and broken and oddly faithful, counting not time exactly, but the intervals in which nothing worse happened.

Everett placed both hands flat on the table.

That was how the morning began.
